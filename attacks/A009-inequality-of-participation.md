# A009 - Inequality of Participation

## Status

Reviewed in paired Phase 3 review. Improvements integrated in [[75_PARTICIPATION_EQUITY_INDICATORS_AND_A009_RESOLUTION|docs/75_PARTICIPATION_EQUITY_INDICATORS_AND_A009_RESOLUTION.md]] and propagated into the core corpus.

## Description

The architecture relies on citizen funding, support, objection, complaint, evidence, following, and delegation. This attack asks whether people with more time, money, education, organization, digital capacity, or social confidence dominate participation.

## Problem

Distributed systems often increase voice for active participants but not necessarily for everyone. If participation capacity is unequal, allocation and complaint visibility may skew toward organized groups.

Example:

```text
Sports clubs quickly mobilize funding and support for Macul courts.
Older adults nearby do not use the platform and do not object to accessibility omissions before funding closes.
```

## Recommended resolution path

- Preserve layered simplicity for ordinary actions.
- Support participation-assistance projects while preventing hidden delegation purchase.
- Make participation concentration and underrepresented affected-party gaps visible where material and privacy-safe.
- Keep delegation revocable and reported.
- Add assisted deliberation that shows dissent and missing voices without deciding for citizens.
- Treat advanced participation-equity measurement as implementation or Extension v1+ observability, not as a universal Core v0 gate.

## Theoretical base and citations

- Sherry Arnstein, "A Ladder of Citizen Participation" (1969): participation can be symbolic unless citizens have real influence.
- Archon Fung, "Varieties of Participation in Complex Governance" (2006): who participates and how participation links to authority matters.
- James Fishkin, `When the People Speak` (2009): deliberative participation can improve quality but requires designed inclusion.
- Mancur Olson, `The Logic of Collective Action` (1965): organized groups can outperform diffuse publics.
- Robert Dahl, `Polyarchy` (1971): broad participation and contestation are both democratic requirements.

## Social reasons

Low-participation groups are often the people most affected by public failures: vulnerable beneficiaries, caregivers, low-income workers, older adults, rural communities, and people with disabilities.

## Conflicts of interest

- Delegates may gather influence from less active citizens without meaningful reporting.
- Participation-support organizations may become indirect political machines.
- Project proponents may mobilize beneficiaries selectively.
- Platform design may favor digitally skilled users.

## Inconsistencies to test

- Verified identity improves accountability, but may deter vulnerable critics.
- Simplicity reduces burden, but can hide important choices.
- Delegation expands participation capacity, but can concentrate influence.

## Stress scenario

The system shows strong funding and support for a project, but most affected parties never saw the project because they are not regular platform users.

## Review checklist

- Are affected-party gaps visible before high-impact decisions?
- Are participation-support projects transparent and non-capturing?
- Does assisted deliberation expose missing voices?
- Can protected identity support vulnerable participation?
- Is delegation reported in ways ordinary citizens actually understand?

## Expected resolution output

A Phase 3 resolution should define a participation-equity boundary: Core v0 should improve the current weak participation baseline through asynchronous visibility, protected identity, delegation transparency, participation-support projects, and threshold-policy participation evidence where required, without turning Core v0 into a full social-policy engine or requiring perfect representativeness before projects can proceed.
