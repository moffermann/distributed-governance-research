# A015 - Common-Good Charter Conflicts

## Status

Prepared for Phase 3 review. Not resolved.

## Description

Core v0 does not include full common-good charter governance, but projects must declare common-good impacts where relevant. This attack asks whether lightweight declarations are enough when projects affect rivers, parks, public spaces, schools, infrastructure, ecosystems, or cultural assets.

## Problem

A project can affect a common good even if no full charter exists. If the platform lacks registry mechanics, compatibility rules, or affected-party mapping, important impacts may be underdeclared or discovered too late.

Example:

```text
The Macul courts improve sports access but reduce park green area, increase noise, or restrict informal public use.
No active common-good charter exists, so the project proceeds with only a weak declaration.
```

## Recommended resolution path

- Keep full charter governance as Extension v1+, but strengthen Core v0 impact declaration.
- Require affected assets, affected parties, antivalues, risks, evidence needs, and fiscalization path where common-good impact is plausible.
- Allow "uncertain" only with explanation and follow-up review trigger.
- Add affected-party observation and complaint paths.
- Treat undeclared material common-good impact as potential responsibility and reputation input after review.

## Theoretical base and citations

- Elinor Ostrom, `Governing the Commons` (1990): common-pool resources require boundaries, monitoring, sanctions, and local conflict resolution.
- Mancur Olson, `The Logic of Collective Action` (1965): diffuse common-good interests are underrepresented.
- John Rawls, `Political Liberalism` (1993): public reasoning is needed when shared social terms are affected.
- Jurgen Habermas, `Between Facts and Norms` (1996): legitimacy depends on public justification in shared matters.
- Wallace Oates, `Fiscal Federalism` (1972): local decisions may have spillovers requiring broader coordination.

## Social reasons

Common goods often belong to people who are not organized as a project constituency: future users, neighbors, ecosystems, cultural communities, and future generations.

## Conflicts of interest

- Proposers may underdeclare impacts to avoid burden.
- Beneficiaries may ignore costs borne by neighbors.
- Authorities may use common-good language to block competitors.
- Environmental or heritage claims may be used strategically without evidence.

## Inconsistencies to test

- Core v0 excludes full charter governance, but still requires meaningful common-good declarations.
- AI can suggest impacts, but cannot decide compatibility.
- Lack of charter does not block projects, but it can leave no clear standard.

## Stress scenario

A plaza renovation project removes informal sports areas used by teenagers. The project declares "community improvement" but not displacement of existing users.

## Review checklist

- Are affected assets and parties identified?
- Are antivalues and evidence needs defined?
- Is "uncertain" overused?
- Can affected parties raise issues without formal charter machinery?
- Are omissions connected to fiscalization, complaints, and responsibility?

## Expected resolution output

A Phase 3 resolution should define a minimum common-good impact sufficiency test for Core v0.
