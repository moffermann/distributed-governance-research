# A001 - Legitimacy Does Not Follow From Funding

## Status

Reviewed in paired Phase 3 review. Improvements integrated in [[67_PROJECT_LEGITIMACY_PROFILE_AND_A001_RESOLUTION|docs/67_PROJECT_LEGITIMACY_PROFILE_AND_A001_RESOLUTION.md]].

## Description

The architecture allows citizens to commit public-purpose resources to projects. The attack asks whether funding, support, delegation, and visibility could be mistaken for democratic legitimacy, especially when a project affects rights, common goods, vulnerable beneficiaries, or non-participating affected parties.

## Problem

The model says that funding does not substitute for legitimacy, execution readiness, disbursement approval, or fulfillment proof. The failure mode is that users, implementers, or public authorities may still treat funding volume as a legitimacy proxy.

Example:

```text
"Design and Construction of Multi-court Facility in Macul" receives full funding quickly.
Residents near the site argue that access, noise, bathrooms, accessibility, and school-use conflicts were not properly deliberated.
If the system treats funding closure as social approval, affected parties are bypassed.
```

## Recommended resolution path

- Reaffirm that `FundingCommitment`, `SupportSignal`, `Delegation`, and discovery visibility are separate signals.
- Require a legitimacy profile for projects with rights, access, common-good, vulnerable-beneficiary, or territory-wide effects.
- Keep the ordinary citizen action simple, but expose missing legitimacy conditions as plain warnings.
- Route disputed legitimacy into Planning Scope review, affected-party channels, complaint paths, or tutored Governance Resolution where applicable.
- Preserve the distinction between platform consequences and legal/public authority consequences.

## Theoretical base and citations

- Jurgen Habermas, `Between Facts and Norms` (1996): legitimacy depends on public reasoning, not only aggregation.
- John Rawls, `Political Liberalism` (1993): public reason matters when coercive or public rules affect plural citizens.
- Robert Dahl, `Polyarchy` (1971): participation and contestation are both required for democratic legitimacy.
- Sherry Arnstein, "A Ladder of Citizen Participation" (1969): participation can be tokenistic if influence is not real.
- Archon Fung, "Varieties of Participation in Complex Governance" (2006): participatory designs vary by who participates, how they communicate, and how decisions link to authority.
- Mark Bovens, "Analysing and Assessing Accountability" (2007): accountability requires answerability and consequences.
- Elinor Ostrom, `Governing the Commons` (1990): legitimate local governance depends on clear boundaries, monitoring, graduated sanctions, and conflict-resolution mechanisms.

## Social reasons

Funding-heavy projects can silence people who are poor, absent, busy, digitally excluded, or directly affected but not organized. Legitimacy must protect affected-party voice without turning every project into a plebiscite.

## Conflicts of interest

- Large funders, delegates, or organized groups may frame funding success as consent.
- Proposers may use funding momentum to pressure reviewers.
- Tutored authorities may prefer funded projects that align with their own agenda.

## Inconsistencies to test

- The roadmap says funding does not substitute for legitimacy, but the system still depends on funding closure for project progress.
- Planning Scope Alignment exists, but full roadmap construction is unresolved.
- Support signals can express public interest, but support is reversible and not equivalent to affected-party consent.

## Stress scenario

Macul multi-court project is fully funded by sports clubs outside the immediate neighborhood. Nearby residents object that nighttime noise and access rules were not addressed. The system must decide whether funding closure is enough to proceed.

## Review checklist

- Does every project show which legitimacy conditions are met and which remain disputed?
- Are affected parties represented without giving them an automatic veto?
- Are public guarantees and rights protected outside popularity dynamics?
- Is funding closure visibly separate from project legitimacy?
- Does the audit trail preserve legitimacy objections and responses?

## Expected resolution output

A Phase 3 resolution should define a `Project Legitimacy Profile` or equivalent fields without creating heavy bureaucracy for low-risk projects.
