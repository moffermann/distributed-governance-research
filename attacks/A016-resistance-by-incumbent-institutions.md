# A016 - Resistance by Incumbent Institutions

## Status

Reviewed in paired Phase 3 review. Improvements integrated in `docs/82_INCUMBENT_RESISTANCE_INDICATORS_AND_A016_RESOLUTION.md`.

## Description

The system challenges opaque institutional monopoly power. This attack asks whether incumbent institutions can resist, absorb, co-opt, delay, defund, legally constrain, or symbolically adopt the system while preserving existing control.

## Problem

Incumbents may not need to defeat the model openly. They can shape operating modes, eligibility, funding scope, review delays, data access, treasury integration, legal authority, or public narratives.

Example:

```text
A ministry launches a tutored pilot but opens only narrow scopes, delays independent projects, approves authority-linked operators, and reports the pilot as participatory.
```

## Recommended resolution path

- Treat transition pilots as governance objects with observability.
- Require public operating-mode definitions, scope limits, review windows, timeout policies, Governance Resolutions, and aggregate moderation metrics.
- Separate authority role from internal project roles.
- Preserve external legal authority boundaries without allowing hidden judge-and-party control.
- Compare performance against current institutional baseline using the comparative critique rule.

## Theoretical base and citations

- Douglass North, `Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance` (1990): institutions persist through rules, incentives, and path dependence.
- Oliver Williamson, `The Economic Institutions of Capitalism` (1985): governance structures reflect transaction costs and opportunism.
- George Stigler, "The Theory of Economic Regulation" (1971): institutions can be captured by regulated or interested actors.
- Mancur Olson, `The Rise and Decline of Nations` (1982): distributional coalitions can resist change.
- Elinor Ostrom, `Governing the Commons` (1990): institutional change must fit rule systems and monitoring conditions.

## Social reasons

If incumbents can convert the system into a legitimacy display, citizens may lose trust faster than under the old model because the reform promise becomes part of the deception.

## Conflicts of interest

- Authorities may preserve budgetary discretion.
- Public operators may receive hidden subsidies or privileged data.
- Incumbent contractors may adapt to dominate project roles.
- Platform operators may depend on institutional contracts.

## Inconsistencies to test

- The model accepts tutored mode, but tutored mode can become permanent control.
- State-owned operators can participate in non-conflicted modes, but control chains are hard to verify.
- Treasury integration is infrastructure, but can become a political choke point.

## Stress scenario

An incumbent public institution publishes all Governance Resolutions but rejects independent projects with vague "strategic incompatibility" reasons, creating transparency without actual contestability.

## Review checklist

- Are operating-mode boundaries visible?
- Are rejection reasons comparable?
- Is timeout policy meaningful?
- Are authority-linked operators tracked?
- Are aggregate pilot outcomes compared to incumbent baseline?

## Expected resolution output

A Phase 3 resolution should define incumbent-resistance indicators and minimum transition-pilot observability.
