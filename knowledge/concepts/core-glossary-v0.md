# Core Glossary v0

This glossary is a working version. Its purpose is to stabilize the language of the project before writing papers, essays, or implementation proposals.

The terms below are not final definitions. They are operational definitions used to keep the theory coherent while it evolves.

---

## State

A state is an institutional architecture that concentrates certain collective functions: rule creation, dispute resolution, enforcement, defense, public administration, and resource allocation.

In this project, the state is not treated as a single indivisible object. It is decomposed into functions so each function can be evaluated separately.

---

## Distributed State

A distributed state is not a state without rules, without institutions, or without civil order.

It is a state architecture in which some functions currently concentrated in central institutions are redistributed across networks of institutions, citizens, specialists, platforms, auditors, and competing service providers.

The key idea is not institutional disappearance, but functional redistribution.

---

## Distributed Governance

Distributed governance is a governance model in which decision-making, evaluation, accountability, and resource allocation are not concentrated in a single institutional center.

It may include citizens, experts, institutions, arbitration bodies, digital platforms, auditors, and AI-assisted tools.

Distributed governance does not mean that everyone decides everything directly. It means that the capacity to decide, evaluate, audit, correct, and coordinate is distributed across multiple actors.

---

## Functional State Architecture

Functional state architecture is the method of analyzing state functions according to their nature rather than according to inherited institutional categories.

Instead of asking whether the state should be larger or smaller, the framework asks:

- What function must be performed?
- What is the nature of that function?
- Does it depend mainly on force, information, deliberation, trust, coordination, or resource allocation?
- Does centralization reduce risk, or does it create information loss and weak accountability?
- Could distribution improve outcomes?

Core principle:

> The question is not more state or less state. The question is which functions benefit from centralization and which benefit from distribution.

---

## Institution

An institution is an organized mechanism for performing a social function: resolving conflicts, educating, protecting, legislating, caring, auditing, financing, coordinating, or enforcing rules.

Institutions can be public, private, nonprofit, community-based, technical, digital, or hybrid.

This project does not assume that institutions must disappear. It asks which institutions require monopoly and which can operate in competitive, cooperative, auditable networks.

---

## Institutional Monopoly

An institutional monopoly exists when a single institution has exclusive authority, funding, or legitimacy to perform a function, regardless of performance.

The project distinguishes between:

- monopolies that may be functionally justified, such as defense;
- monopolies that may create weak accountability, such as centralized resource allocation ministries.

A central claim of the theory is that many institutional failures are caused less by the existence of institutions and more by monopoly status combined with weak accountability.

---

## Functional Distribution

Functional distribution is the idea that distribution should depend on what the function does.

Functions close to the use of force tend to require stronger central coordination. Functions centered on information, deliberation, evaluation, resource allocation, or service provision have greater potential for distribution.

This avoids the simplistic frame of distributing everything or centralizing everything.

---

## Defense

Defense is the function of protecting a territory or political community from external threats.

In the current framework, defense is one of the strongest candidates for centralization because it requires unified command, doctrine, coordination, and control over force.

Distributed territorial defense may create risks of fragmentation, conflicting chains of command, or civil conflict.

---

## Security

Security is the function of preventing, detecting, responding to, and investigating threats to public order and personal safety.

Unlike defense, security may allow partial functional distribution. The core coercive role may remain centralized or tightly coordinated, while specialized complementary institutions can emerge.

Examples:

- drone monitoring;
- cybercrime units;
- forensic digital services;
- AI-supported crime analysis;
- search and rescue;
- specialized prevention and surveillance.

The key distinction is functional distribution, not uncontrolled competition for the use of force.

---

## Justice

Justice is the function of resolving conflicts, interpreting rules, and determining responsibilities or remedies.

Justice has significant distribution potential because arbitration, mediation, and specialized tribunals already exist.

The main challenge is legitimacy and jurisdiction: parties must know which institution is competent, under what rules, and how decisions are enforced.

A distributed justice architecture may include multiple arbiters operating under a shared legal framework.

---

## Legislative Function

The legislative function is the creation, modification, and evaluation of common rules.

It has high distribution potential because legislation is fundamentally deliberative and informational, not directly coercive at the drafting stage.

Citizens, specialists, representatives, and multiple drafting teams could propose, review, debate, simulate, and vote on legal changes through digital platforms.

This does not necessarily eliminate legislators. It changes the architecture of lawmaking so it becomes more participatory, transparent, and knowledge-rich.

---

## Executive Resource Allocation

Executive resource allocation is the function currently performed by many ministries that decide how to distribute public resources across areas such as education, health, culture, sports, science, and social development.

This project treats this function as highly distributable.

The hypothesis is that centralized ministries may no longer be necessary as primary resource allocators where technology allows citizens and communities to assign resources directly to institutions and projects that demonstrate value.

Schools, hospitals, foundations, research centers, and cultural organizations would not disappear. They would compete for support by demonstrating results.

---

## Distributed Resource Allocation

Distributed resource allocation is a mechanism in which many people assign resources directly to projects, institutions, or causes they consider valuable.

The algorithm does not decide what has value. It coordinates and records many human decisions.

In this model, funding follows demonstrated value, reputation, evidence, and citizen support rather than only centralized budget decisions.

---

## Value

Value is not limited to economic return.

A project generates value if it improves human or social conditions. Examples include:

- educating better;
- caring better;
- saving lives;
- reducing suffering;
- reducing poverty;
- strengthening a community;
- producing knowledge;
- increasing opportunities;
- improving culture;
- solving real problems.

Because value is partly subjective and partly measurable, the theory does not require a single authority or algorithm to define value. It allows many people and institutions to express, compare, and verify value through distributed signals.

---

## Algorithm as Coordinator

An algorithm as coordinator does not decide for people. It records, aggregates, structures, and executes decisions made by people.

Analogy:

A social network does not decide which post a person likes, comments on, or shares. The person decides. The platform coordinates millions of interactions.

In a governance system:

- posts become projects;
- likes or interactions become resource assignments;
- people decide what they value;
- the system coordinates the result.

---

## Algorithm as Decider

An algorithm as decider evaluates options and determines outcomes centrally.

This model is risky because it may recreate the same concentration of power the theory is trying to avoid.

The project generally prefers algorithms as coordinators, not sovereign decision-makers.

---

## Distributed Trust

Distributed trust is the principle that trust should emerge from verification, auditability, traceability, reputation, and multiple independent actors rather than blind dependence on one central authority.

Key phrase:

> We do not need to trust whoever publishes the information. We need to be able to verify it.

Distributed trust can apply to information, institutions, contracts, funding, arbitration, and governance.

---

## Information Integrity

Information integrity means that claims, metrics, documents, and results can be traced, audited, challenged, and compared.

A distributed system should avoid replacing a political monopoly with an information monopoly.

Information integrity requires:

- multiple sources;
- public evidence;
- audit trails;
- independent verification;
- reputation of sources;
- ability to contest false claims.

---

## Assisted Deliberation

Assisted deliberation is the use of technology, including AI, to improve the quality of human decision-making without replacing human judgment.

Before voting or assigning resources, the system should help users understand:

- pros and cons;
- costs;
- externalities;
- risks;
- alternatives;
- affected groups;
- contextualized evidence quality;
- expert disagreement;
- historical performance.

The goal is not to make people obey an algorithm. The goal is to make decisions better informed.

---

## Personal AI Guide

A personal AI guide is an AI system aligned with an individual’s preferences, values, goals, and decision patterns.

Its role is to reduce the cognitive cost of participation by summarizing proposals, identifying contradictions, comparing options, and helping the person understand how a decision relates to their own principles.

It should not be confused with a central AI that decides for everyone.

---

## Performance Dashboard

A performance dashboard is a visible summary of an institution’s historical commitments and results.

It may include:

- goals promised;
- budget requested;
- budget executed;
- KPIs;
- independent audits;
- beneficiary evaluations;
- complaints;
- evidence of execution;
- comparison with similar institutions.

The purpose is to help citizens allocate resources based on verifiable history, not only promises or rhetoric.

---

## Distributed Accountability Loop

Distributed accountability means that project performance does not depend only on the actor receiving resources reporting its own success.

Accountability should combine:

- self-declared commitments;
- fulfillment/control evidence;
- financial traceability;
- beneficiary feedback;
- family or user evaluations;
- independent auditors;
- experts;
- complaints;
- formal evaluation records;
- fiscalization reports;
- project closure accountability records;
- AI-assisted inconsistency detection.

Core cycle:

> proposal → financing → execution → fulfillment/control evidence → formal evaluation → closure accountability → reputation → new financing

A project cannot close as fulfilled if its main commitments are not supported by sufficient, reviewed, traceable fulfillment/control evidence. Weak or inconclusive material may remain contextual, but it does not prove fulfillment.

---

## Fiscalization / Oversight

Fiscalization is the process of checking whether institutions actually did what they promised and whether resources were used properly.

In a distributed system, oversight should also be distributed. It should not depend only on the institution being evaluated or a single central authority.

The challenge is to design enough oversight to prevent fraud without creating a new bureaucratic monopoly.

---

## Representation

Representation means that someone acts, decides, or speaks on behalf of another person or group.

In the current state model, children, dependent adults, and many vulnerable groups are represented by parents, guardians, judges, agencies, ministries, or institutions.

A distributed model does not eliminate representation. It asks whether representation can become more plural, transparent, auditable, and contestable.

---

## Legitimacy

Legitimacy is the accepted right of an institution, rule, or decision process to produce binding outcomes.

In a centralized state, legitimacy often comes from constitutional authority, elections, legal hierarchy, and monopoly enforcement.

In a distributed architecture, legitimacy may also emerge from transparency, prior consent, performance, auditability, reputation, and participation.

This is one of the most important open problems in the theory.

---

## Accountability

Accountability is the capacity to evaluate, question, sanction, replace, or stop supporting an institution or actor based on performance.

A major critique of institutional monopolies is that poor performance often has weak consequences.

Distributed governance seeks stronger accountability by making results visible and allowing support to move toward better-performing institutions.

---

## Institutional Competition

Institutional competition means that multiple institutions can attempt to solve similar problems and attract legitimacy, users, or resources by demonstrating better results.

This is not merely price competition. It is competition over quality, trust, value, accountability, and outcomes.

Institutional competition is most appropriate where fragmentation does not create unacceptable coercive or systemic risks.

---

## Institutional Cooperation

Institutional cooperation means that multiple institutions coordinate around shared standards, information, protocols, and legal frameworks while remaining distinct.

A distributed system should not be understood only as competition. It also requires cooperation, interoperability, and shared rules.

---

## Centralization

Centralization means concentrating authority, decision-making, information, funding, or enforcement in a single institution or small group of institutions.

Centralization may be beneficial when coordination failure is more dangerous than monopoly failure, especially in defense or core coercive security.

---

## Distribution

Distribution means spreading authority, information, evaluation, funding, or execution across multiple actors or institutions.

Distribution may be beneficial when centralization produces information loss, weak accountability, poor incentives, or slow adaptation.

---

## Hybrid Function

A hybrid function is a function that requires both central coordination and distributed execution or evaluation.

Security is currently treated as a likely hybrid function: core coercive authority may require central control, while specialized support functions may be distributed.

Justice may also be hybrid: shared legal framework, multiple arbiters.

---

## Network of Institutions

A network of institutions is a system where multiple organizations perform related functions under shared rules, interoperability standards, auditing mechanisms, and reputation systems.

This concept contrasts with a single monopoly institution.

---

## Centralized Resource Allocator

A centralized resource allocator is an institution that decides how resources are distributed across projects, services, regions, or organizations from a central position.

Many ministries operate partly as centralized resource allocators.

The theory asks whether some of these allocation functions can be replaced by distributed mechanisms.

---

## Augmented Democracy

Augmented democracy is a more direct, more informed, more traceable form of democratic participation supported by technology.

It does not assume that voting systems are perfect. It recognizes the limitations of current democratic mechanisms and asks whether participation can be improved with better information, deliberation, delegation, and accountability.

---

## Social Value Signal

A social value signal is any indicator that a project or institution is creating value.

Examples:

- citizen support;
- beneficiary feedback;
- expert evaluation;
- KPI completion;
- independent audits;
- repeated funding;
- community engagement;
- measurable outcomes;
- reduction in complaints.

No single signal is sufficient. The theory favors combining many signals.

---

## Open Definitions

The following terms still require further work:

- common legal framework;
- distributed legitimacy;
- platform capture;
- minimum central authority;
- institutional interoperability;
- coercive core;
- neglected essential services;
- anti-manipulation mechanisms;
- transition architecture.
