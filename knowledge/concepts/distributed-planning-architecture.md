# Concept — Distributed Planning Architecture

## Definition

Distributed Planning Architecture is the planning layer of the distributed governance model. It defines how a society can maintain a shared long-term roadmap while allowing goals, projects, and revisions to emerge from distributed participation.

## Core claim

Planning should be binding, but not static.

A society needs long-term direction, but that direction should not be monopolized by the government of the moment or reset every electoral cycle.

## Architecture

The architecture has several layers:

```text
meta-planning national framework
  → national planning branches
    → regional planning branches
      → communal / local planning branches
        → projects
```

## Meta-planning

Meta-planning is the common constitutional-like framework of the planning system.

It defines:

- how goals are created;
- how goals are modified;
- how goals are removed;
- how regional and local branches relate to national goals;
- how projects must attach to approved goals;
- how conflicts and obsolescence are handled.

## National, regional, and local planning

A single national roadmap preserves coherence. Regional and local branches allow each territory to express its own priorities while remaining connected to the national framework.

A project should be placed at the level corresponding to its scope:

- national projects under national goals;
- regional projects under regional goals;
- communal or local projects under local goals.

## Project attachment

Projects should not float independently. They must attach to an approved roadmap goal.

Each project declares:

- one Primary Responsibility Anchor;
- optional secondary contributions;
- scope level;
- expected contribution;
- KPIs;
- budget;
- timeline.

The Primary Responsibility Anchor is the project's main accountability center. It identifies the roadmap goal, public function, or public-value outcome against which the project is primarily classified, compared, funded, evaluated, fiscalized, and held accountable.

Secondary contributions are allowed, but they do not replace the anchor. If a secondary contribution is presented as a formal promise, it should have proportional metrics, fulfillment/control evidence, and review consequences.

If several outcomes are each independently primary, the proposal should be challenged as either:

- separate projects;
- project phases under one parent anchor where the work is sequential and serves the same result;
- a composite program where several child projects are genuinely interdependent.

## Living roadmap

The roadmap grows organically. Universities, companies, foundations, citizens, communities, or institutions can propose additions or modifications.

However, proposals do not become binding automatically. They require deliberation, support, and defined approval mechanisms.

## Goal lifecycle

Goals can be:

```text
Active
Underfunded
Under Review
At Risk
Reformulated
Suspended
Eliminated
Completed
```

## Planning conflicts

Conflicts between goals should be made visible rather than hidden inside political discretion.

Example:

- reduce CO2 emissions;
- expand lithium mining.

The system should expose the conflict and invite projects that reduce the tradeoff, such as cleaner mining, electrification, carbon capture, or compensation mechanisms.

## Key insight

Not all contradictions need to be solved from above. Some should be made visible so society can deliberate and innovate around them.

## Relation to current systems

Current political systems also face conflicting priorities, but they often resolve them through opaque political discretion, budget negotiations, lobbying, or executive preference.

A distributed planning architecture should be judged comparatively: can it handle these conflicts at least as well as the current model, with more transparency and better feedback?
