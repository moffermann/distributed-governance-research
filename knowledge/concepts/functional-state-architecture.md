# Concept — Functional State Architecture

Functional State Architecture means analyzing the state by function and functional layer rather than by inherited institutional form.

## Core idea

Instead of asking whether the state should be larger or smaller, ask:

- What function must be performed?
- What is the nature of the function?
- Which layer is being analyzed: public guarantee, civil rights, standards, accreditation, coercion, rules, planning, funding, service provision, execution, evidence, fiscalization, evaluation, enforcement, rights protection, emergency response, data custody, or accountability?
- Does that layer depend on force, information, deliberation, trust, coordination, legality, standards, or resource allocation?
- Is the question about defining a public/legal guarantee, or about who provides the service?
- What degree of centralization, hybridity, or distribution minimizes risk and maximizes value for that layer?

## Why it matters

A single ideological answer cannot fit all state functions. Defense, policing, courts, legislation, education funding, health care, culture, and sports are not the same kind of problem.

Even inside one sector, layers differ. Security may need centralized coercive authority while allowing distributed prevention, victim support, evidence, and oversight. Health may need common legal guarantees, patient rights, accreditation, triage standards, payment rules, and data interoperability while allowing urgent care, clinics, prevention, evidence, and fiscalization to be provided by eligible public, private, nonprofit, cooperative, university, concession-like, or state-owned operators.

## Architectural principle

Centralize layers where fragmentation creates unacceptable risks. Distribute layers where centralization creates information loss, weak accountability, monopoly power, weak contestability, or poor incentives. Use hybrid architectures where common rules, standards, rights protection, financing rules, legal consequences, or coordination must coexist with distributed service provision, execution, evidence, evaluation, or resource allocation.

Centralizing the legal framework is not the same as centralizing service delivery. A public guarantee can be legally centralized while service delivery is distributed under accreditation, evidence, fiscalization, contracts, reputation, and applicable legal sanctions.
