# Concept — Distributed Trust

Distributed trust is confidence produced by architecture rather than by blind dependence on a single authority or by faith in many actors.

In Core v0, trust emerges when material claims are:

- attributable to an actor and role;
- linked to contextualized evidence;
- open to corroboration and contradiction;
- reviewed by the responsible fiscalizer, reviewer, authority, or protocol path where formal effects are involved;
- connected to payment, disbursement, reputation, responsibility, correction, revocation, or other proportional consequences;
- preserved in an audit trail.

## Key phrase

> Trust is not created by believing more actors. It is created when the system aligns incentives and makes claims verifiable, contestable, reviewable, and consequential.

## Core chain

```text
Material Claim
→ contextualized evidence
→ corroboration / contradiction / complaint path
→ responsible review
→ EvaluationRecord where formal effect exists
→ accountability, reputation, payment, correction, or responsibility consequence
→ citizen-facing trust signal
→ audit trail
```

## Incentive component

Verification alone is not enough.

Actors must have reasons to produce reliable behavior:

- evidence producers are paid and reputationally evaluated for useful, sufficient, traceable evidence;
- fiscalizers gain or lose reputation based on review quality and independence;
- executors receive disbursements only through milestone, evidence, and review conditions;
- verified discoveries may be recognized when they materially improve information quality;
- false, manipulated, hidden, or low-quality information can create role-specific responsibility.

Without this incentive layer, distributed trust becomes only distributed faith.

## Applications

- information integrity;
- public spending;
- project value and antivalue verification;
- fulfillment/control evidence;
- fiscalization;
- complaints and contradiction paths;
- role-based reputation;
- custody and disbursement;
- governance processes;
- private agreements or arbitration-like systems as a related future application.

## Blockchain analogy

Blockchain is a useful analogy for registry integrity because it reduces dependence on one trusted record keeper.

But registry integrity is not truth.

A preserved falsehood remains false. Core v0 therefore treats tamper-resistance as possible infrastructure, while trust itself comes from incentive alignment, evidence, contradiction, review, consequences, and auditability.
