# Project Governance Glossary Addendum

This addendum extends `core-glossary-v0.md` with concepts discovered during the project governance, antivalue, reputation, and fiscalization discussion.

## Antivalue

Antivalue is the negative social effect generated by a project. It may include pollution, noise, health damage, environmental degradation, fraud, abuse, risk, displacement, loss of quality of life, or other harms.

Antivalue is related to externalities, but the term is broader because it connects directly to the theory's concept of social value.

## Net Social Value

Net social value is the perceived balance between the value a project creates and the antivalue it generates.

```text
value generated - antivalue generated = perceived net social value
```

The system does not require a central authority to compute this perfectly. It requires transparency so society can evaluate the tradeoff.

## Mitigation Project

A mitigation project is a project whose primary goal is to reduce the antivalue created by another project.

Example: a plant generates jobs but also bad odors. A mitigation project may install treatment technology, improve waste systems, or measure emissions.

## Pause

A pause is a temporary protective stop applied to a project when relevant antivalue or serious noncompliance appears.

A pause is not the same as revocation. It creates time for evidence, mitigation, fiscalization, and social validation.

## Revocation

Revocation is the termination of a project's authorization, funding, or legitimacy inside the system.

Revocation should be more demanding than pause because it destroys or ends the project rather than temporarily suspending it.

## Financial Assurance

Financial assurance is the set of mechanisms that ensures projects can respond when they fail or generate undeclared antivalue.

Examples:

- performance bonds;
- guarantees;
- insurance;
- retained funds;
- escrow-like custody;
- milestone-based disbursement.

## Fiscalizer

A fiscalizer is an actor that measures, audits, inspects, or verifies whether a project fulfilled its commitments.

Fiscalizers can be companies, universities, laboratories, technical experts, auditors, civil society organizations, or other specialized institutions.

## Fiscalization Ecosystem

A fiscalization ecosystem is a distributed network of fiscalizers that are themselves transparent, auditable, reputable, and replaceable.

## Observability

Observability means that an actor has direct or verifiable access to a project dimension and therefore can meaningfully evaluate it.

If an actor cannot observe or verify a dimension, they may express an opinion or alert, but should not formally score that dimension.

## Role-Based Reputation

Role-based reputation tracks the performance of people and institutions according to their actual responsibility in projects.

A project lead, technical lead, financial auditor, legal representative, and beneficiary should not all affect reputation in the same way.

## Time-Weighted Reputation

Time-weighted reputation gives more weight to recent performance and gradually reduces the weight of older events.

This allows recovery from failure without erasing historical traceability.

## Functional Bankruptcy

Functional bankruptcy is the principle that institutions managing social resources should be able to lose funding, reputation, continuity, or operational existence if they fail to deliver the value they promised or transfer undeclared costs to society.

The goal is not to protect institutions, but to protect the value society expected from them.
