# Functional Matrix v0

This matrix is a first baseline for classifying state functions according to their functional nature and likely degree of centralization or distribution.

It is not a final model. It is a working tool for discussion.

## Core design question

For each function, ask:

> If we remove the inherited institutional form, what minimum function remains, and what architecture solves it best?

## Scale

- **Centralized:** strong central authority is functionally justified.
- **Hybrid:** central rules or coordination remain, but execution, evaluation, or support functions can be distributed.
- **Distributed:** the function can largely be performed through distributed institutions, projects, platforms, and accountability mechanisms.

# 1. Defense

## Minimum function

Protect the political community or territory against external threats.

## Functional nature

Defense is fundamentally tied to organized use of force, unified command, intelligence, logistics, doctrine, and rapid coordination.

## Risk of centralization

- Strategic mistakes by a small leadership group.
- Political capture of armed forces.
- Bureaucratic inefficiency.

## Risk of distribution

- Fragmented command.
- Conflicting armed groups.
- Civil conflict.
- Inability to coordinate under external threat.

## Suggested architecture

**Centralized.**

Defense is currently the strongest case for institutional centralization. The use of organized force against external threats probably requires a unified chain of command.

## Open questions

- How can centralized defense be made more accountable without weakening operational command?
- What oversight mechanisms should exist over defense institutions?

# 2. Security Public Order

## Minimum function

Prevent, detect, respond to, and investigate threats to public order and personal safety.

## Functional nature

Security combines coercive authority, prevention, information, investigation, monitoring, and emergency response.

## Risk of centralization

- Abuse of authority.
- Slow innovation.
- Poor specialization.
- Weak accountability.
- Lack of local knowledge.

## Risk of distribution

- Competing claims over legitimate force.
- Conflicting operational authority.
- Private coercive capture.
- Unequal protection across territories.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid.**

The coercive core should remain centralized or tightly coordinated. However, many functions can be distributed functionally rather than territorially:

- drone monitoring;
- AI-supported crime analysis;
- cybercrime investigation;
- forensic digital services;
- missing-person search;
- emergency dispatch support;
- prevention analytics;
- non-coercive community monitoring.

## Key distinction

The model should not create many competing police forces using coercive power. It should allow specialized institutions to complement the core security function under clear legal roles.

## Open questions

- Which security functions require coercive authority?
- Which can be performed by specialized non-coercive institutions?
- How should accountability work when private or nonprofit actors support public safety?

# 3. Justice

## Minimum function

Resolve conflicts, interpret rules, assign responsibility, and define remedies under a shared legal framework.

## Functional nature

Justice is rule interpretation plus legitimacy. It requires procedural fairness, evidence evaluation, enforceability, and accepted jurisdiction.

## Risk of centralization

- Slow courts.
- High cost.
- Lack of specialization.
- Monopoly and weak service accountability.
- Distance from users.

## Risk of distribution

- Competing jurisdictions.
- Conflicting decisions.
- Unequal access.
- Forum shopping.
- Disputes over legitimacy.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid/distributed.**

Justice can support multiple arbiters, mediators, and specialized tribunals under a shared legal framework. This already exists partially through private arbitration and mediation.

## Possible model

- Common civil/legal framework.
- Multiple certified arbitration institutions.
- Prior agreement between parties on jurisdiction where possible.
- Public fallback court for cases without agreed jurisdiction or involving fundamental rights.
- Transparent records of decisions and institutional performance.

## Open questions

- How is jurisdiction assigned when parties did not agree in advance?
- Which cases must remain in public courts?
- How are distributed arbitral decisions enforced?

# 4. Legislative Function

## Minimum function

Create, revise, repeal, and interpret common rules for the community.

## Functional nature

Legislation is deliberation, knowledge aggregation, value conflict, drafting quality, representation, and legitimacy.

## Risk of centralization

- Capture by political parties or interest groups.
- Low citizen participation.
- Low technical quality.
- Distance from affected people.
- Slow adaptation.

## Risk of distribution

- Populist or contradictory lawmaking.
- Excessive proposal volume.
- Low drafting quality.
- Manipulation of public opinion.
- Difficulty maintaining legal coherence.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid/distributed.**

The legislative function has high distribution potential because lawmaking does not require direct use of force at the deliberation and drafting stages.

## Possible model

- Citizens can propose rules.
- Experts can review and annotate proposals.
- Multiple drafting teams can produce alternative versions.
- AI can detect contradictions, costs, affected laws, and implementation risks.
- Representatives may remain but with more direct citizen input.
- Popular voting can occur in defined instances.

## Open questions

- How are low-quality or duplicate proposals filtered?
- What level of technical review is mandatory?
- How should expert input affect final decisions?
- How do we prevent legal fragmentation?

# 5. Education Ecosystem

## Minimum function

Enable learning, development, literacy, skills, social mobility, and opportunity.

## Functional nature

Education is a service ecosystem involving families, students, teachers, schools, projects, standards, evaluation, and long-term social value.

## Current institutional form

A ministry often designs programs, allocates resources, sets standards, manages priorities, and funds institutions.

## Reframed question

If the ministry no longer acts as central designer and allocator, what remains necessary?

## Residual common functions

- Protect children’s rights.
- Define minimum educational standards.
- Maintain comparable public information.
- Prevent fraud.
- Ensure some access floor.
- Validate basic credentials.
- Track learning outcomes.

## Distributable functions

Many current ministry tasks can become projects:

- improving attendance;
- early reading;
- university access;
- teacher training;
- school innovation;
- mental health support;
- tutoring;
- curriculum experiments;
- infrastructure improvements;
- technology adoption.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed with minimal guarantees.**

Schools, foundations, companies, communities, and educational projects compete for support by demonstrating value. The state-like layer becomes a guarantor of standards, rights, information integrity, and anti-fraud mechanisms rather than a central designer of solutions.

## Open questions

- How is minimum access guaranteed?
- How are children represented?
- How do we prevent only visible or emotionally attractive projects from being funded?
- How do we measure education quality without reducing it to narrow KPIs?

# 6. Health Ecosystem

## Minimum function

Improve health outcomes through prevention, diagnosis, treatment, care, and emergency response.

## Functional nature

Health combines technical expertise, service provision, risk pooling, urgency, standards, and equity concerns.

## Risk of centralization

- Waiting lists.
- Poor accountability.
- Bureaucratic allocation.
- Low innovation.
- Weak responsiveness to patients.

## Risk of distribution

- Unequal access.
- Cream-skimming.
- Fragmented medical records.
- Poor continuity of care.
- Incentives to avoid costly patients.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid/distributed.**

Service provision, innovation projects, clinics, prevention programs, and specialized care may be distributed. However, standards, emergency access, patient rights, health data interoperability, and some risk-pooling mechanisms likely require central or shared coordination.

## Open questions

- How should high-cost patients be protected?
- What must be universally guaranteed?
- How do distributed providers interoperate?
- What health outcomes are legitimate performance signals?

# 7. Culture

## Minimum function

Enable creation, preservation, access, and participation in cultural life.

## Functional nature

Culture is highly plural, subjective, community-based, and difficult to value through a single central criterion.

## Risk of centralization

- Committees define culture from above.
- Political favoritism.
- Homogenization.
- Funding captured by well-connected groups.

## Risk of distribution

- Popularity bias.
- Low-visibility heritage may be neglected.
- Short-term trends may dominate.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed.**

Culture is a strong candidate for distributed allocation because value is plural and subjective. Citizens and communities can directly support cultural projects they consider valuable.

## Open questions

- How do we protect cultural heritage with low mass appeal?
- Should there be reserved funds for preservation?
- How do we avoid pure popularity dynamics?

# 8. Sports

## Minimum function

Promote physical activity, community participation, health, talent development, and competitive excellence.

## Functional nature

Sports involve community projects, infrastructure, coaching, clubs, events, and measurable outcomes.

## Risk of centralization

- Funding based on political criteria.
- Poor local knowledge.
- Support for federations without accountability.

## Risk of distribution

- Popular sports absorb most funding.
- Less visible disciplines are neglected.
- Elite and grassroots priorities may conflict.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed.**

Clubs, federations, schools, municipalities, and community projects can compete for support through participation outcomes, health outcomes, talent development, and community value.

## Open questions

- How do minority sports survive?
- How should elite sport and grassroots sport be balanced?
- What performance metrics are fair?

# 9. Science / Research

## Minimum function

Generate knowledge, solve complex problems, and support long-term discovery.

## Functional nature

Science combines technical expertise, uncertainty, long time horizons, peer evaluation, and public goods.

## Risk of centralization

- Bureaucratic capture.
- Politicized funding.
- Conservatism and low innovation.
- Slow adaptation to emerging fields.

## Risk of distribution

- Basic research may be underfunded.
- Popular science may crowd out fundamental work.
- Citizens may not be able to evaluate technical merit.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid.**

Science should combine distributed support, expert review, open peer evaluation, and protected long-term funding for basic research.

## Possible model

- Citizen-supported research funds.
- Expert panels with transparent scoring.
- Open peer review.
- Research performance dashboards.
- Protected funds for long-horizon research.

## Open questions

- How do we prevent underfunding of basic research?
- How much weight should experts have?
- How should failed experiments be valued?

# 10. Social Development / Poverty Reduction

## Minimum function

Reduce poverty, vulnerability, exclusion, and human suffering.

## Functional nature

Social development is service delivery, case management, resource allocation, human complexity, and impact evaluation.

## Risk of centralization

- Programs persist without results.
- Clientelism.
- Poor targeting.
- Low accountability.
- Distance from lived reality.

## Risk of distribution

- Fraud.
- Duplicated efforts.
- Neglect of hard cases.
- Emotional manipulation.
- Volatile funding.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed with strong accountability.**

Foundations, local organizations, companies, and social projects can compete for support by demonstrating impact. This requires strong dashboards, beneficiary validation, audits, and anti-fraud mechanisms.

## Open questions

- How are the hardest cases protected if they are expensive and difficult?
- How do we avoid funding only emotionally compelling causes?
- How do we measure impact without oversimplifying poverty?

# 11. Elderly Care

## Minimum function

Protect and care for elderly people, especially those with dependency, illness, or abandonment.

## Functional nature

Care provision, rights protection, family involvement, service quality, and vulnerability safeguards.

## Risk of centralization

- Low service quality.
- Institutional neglect.
- Lack of family visibility.
- Bureaucratic standards without real care.

## Risk of distribution

- Abuse by providers.
- False reporting.
- Unequal quality.
- Neglect of people without family advocates.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed with strong oversight.**

Care homes, foundations, local providers, and specialized institutions can receive support based on verified care quality. Oversight must include beneficiaries where possible, families, independent audits, inspections, complaints, and outcome tracking.

## Open questions

- Who represents elderly people without family?
- How is abuse detected?
- Which quality indicators actually reflect dignified care?

# 12. Childhood Protection

## Minimum function

Protect children from harm and ensure basic conditions for development.

## Functional nature

Rights protection, care, guardianship, education, health, family context, and vulnerability.

## Risk of centralization

- Institutional abuse hidden inside bureaucracies.
- Slow response.
- Poor case follow-up.
- Weak accountability.

## Risk of distribution

- Inconsistent standards.
- Private abuse.
- Conflicting guardianship claims.
- Difficulty representing children’s interests.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid.**

Children require stronger rights guarantees than ordinary service consumers because they cannot fully represent themselves. Service provision can be distributed, but standards, oversight, legal protection, and emergency intervention require strong common rules.

## Open questions

- Who represents the child in resource allocation systems?
- Which decisions must remain under court or guardian oversight?
- How are providers audited?

# 13. Housing

## Minimum function

Enable access to shelter, neighborhood integration, and dignified living conditions.

## Functional nature

Housing combines land, finance, construction, regulation, urban planning, infrastructure, and social need.

## Risk of centralization

- Slow delivery.
- Political allocation.
- Poor local fit.
- Standardized solutions that ignore real needs.

## Risk of distribution

- Speculation.
- Segregation.
- Unequal access.
- Local resistance to projects.
- Poor urban coordination.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid/distributed.**

Project design, financing, construction, and community initiatives can be distributed. Land-use rules, minimum standards, urban coordination, and anti-fraud mechanisms likely require shared governance.

## Open questions

- How do distributed housing projects coordinate with city planning?
- How are vulnerable families prioritized?
- How do we prevent capture by real estate interests?

# 14. Local Infrastructure

## Minimum function

Build and maintain local public goods such as roads, parks, lighting, community centers, drainage, and public spaces.

## Functional nature

Infrastructure is capital investment, maintenance, local knowledge, technical standards, and long-term coordination.

## Risk of centralization

- Poor local knowledge.
- Overpriced projects.
- Political prioritization.
- Weak maintenance incentives.

## Risk of distribution

- Fragmented planning.
- Underfunding maintenance.
- Short-term visible projects over hidden infrastructure.
- Local inequality.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid/distributed.**

Project proposals and prioritization can be distributed locally. Technical standards, safety standards, and interoperability with broader networks require common frameworks.

## Open questions

- How do we fund maintenance, not only new projects?
- How do we compare visible and invisible infrastructure?
- How do local projects integrate with regional planning?

# 15. Public Registries

## Minimum function

Maintain authoritative records of identity, property, companies, contracts, permits, and legal status.

## Functional nature

Public registries are information integrity, legal recognition, identity, and trust infrastructure.

## Risk of centralization

- Single point of failure.
- Bureaucratic friction.
- Data abuse.
- Corruption in record changes.

## Risk of distribution

- Conflicting records.
- Identity fraud.
- Legal uncertainty.
- Interoperability failure.

## Suggested architecture

**Hybrid.**

Registries may benefit from distributed technical architecture and public auditability, but require common standards and legal recognition.

## Open questions

- Can registries be technically distributed while legally unified?
- What role should cryptographic verification play?
- How is privacy preserved?

# 16. Oversight / Fiscalization

## Minimum function

Verify that institutions use resources properly and deliver what they promised.

## Functional nature

Oversight is information integrity, auditability, evidence, incentives, and accountability.

## Risk of centralization

- Auditors captured by politics.
- Limited capacity.
- Slow detection.
- Hidden failures.

## Risk of distribution

- Low-quality auditors.
- Conflicting evaluations.
- Manipulated beneficiary feedback.
- Audit fatigue.

## Suggested architecture

**Distributed/hybrid.**

Oversight should not depend only on the institution receiving resources or on a single central auditor. It should combine self-reporting, evidence, beneficiaries, families, users, experts, independent auditors, public complaints, and AI-assisted inconsistency detection.

## Core cycle

> proposal → financing → execution → evidence → evaluation → reputation → new financing

## Open questions

- How much fiscalization is enough?
- Who audits the auditors?
- How do we prevent metric manipulation?
- How do we avoid creating a new bureaucracy of oversight?
