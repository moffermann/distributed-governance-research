# H001 — Functional Layer Distribution Principle

## Hypothesis

The optimal degree of institutional distribution depends on the functional layer being analyzed, not on a general ideological preference for more state, less state, centralization, or decentralization.

The relevant unit is not always the inherited institution or the broad policy sector. It is often the layer inside a public function: legal guarantee, civil rights, standards, accreditation, coercion, rule creation, eligibility, planning, funding, service provision, execution, evidence, fiscalization, evaluation, enforcement, data custody, rights protection, emergency response, or accountability.

## Working formulation

Layers that define public guarantees, civil rights, mandatory coverage, enforceable standards, accreditation, legitimate force, rights deprivation, legal coercion, or macro fiscal stability tend to require stronger central coordination, common rules, or legally recognized authority.

Layers centered on information, deliberation, knowledge generation, local service provision, project execution, bounded resource allocation, fulfillment evidence, fiscalization, service evaluation, reputation, and public accountability tend to have greater distribution potential.

The same public function can therefore contain centralized, hybrid, and distributed layers at the same time.

Centralized law, rights, standards, funding rules, or public guarantees do not imply centralized state service provision. A service can be provided by eligible public, private, nonprofit, cooperative, university, concession-like, or state-owned operators while remaining subject to a common legal framework, public financing rules, accreditation, evidence, fiscalization, and legal consequences.

## Rationale

Not all state functions share the same risk profile. Fragmenting the operational command of defense may create catastrophic coordination and civil conflict risks. Distributing public deliberation, evidence production, auditing, research review, project execution, or bounded resource allocation may improve information quality, accountability, innovation, local fit, and contestability.

Broad labels such as `security`, `health`, `justice`, or `education` can hide internal variation:

- security contains coercive police authority, prevention, investigation, civic observation, data analysis, victim support, and institutional oversight;
- health contains public guarantees, legal duties, emergency access rules, minimum standards, rights protection, prevention, care delivery, evidence, patient feedback, and funding;
- justice contains enforceable legal authority, but also mediation, evidence, legal aid, case transparency, expert review, and specialized arbitration-like mechanisms;
- education contains minimum rights and standards, but also service delivery, local projects, curriculum experiments, evidence, and performance visibility.

H001 therefore rejects both blanket centralization and blanket distribution. It asks which layer should be centralized, hybrid, or distributed, and why.

## Functional layer test

For each public function, ask:

```text
1. What is the minimum function being performed?
2. Which layer is being analyzed?
3. Is this a public/legal guarantee layer or a service provision layer?
4. Does that layer use or authorize force?
5. Can failure cause irreversible harm?
6. Does it require unified command under time pressure?
7. Does it affect third-party rights or vulnerable beneficiaries?
8. Does it require common standards, accreditation, legal recognition, or enforceability?
9. Can performance be evidenced, audited, compared, corrected, or replaced?
10. Does centralization create information loss, monopoly power, weak accountability, or capture?
11. Does distribution create fragmentation, fraud, inequality, volatility, cream-skimming, or coordination failure?
12. Which failures create platform consequences, which create legal consequences, and which competent authority, regulator, court, contract, or enforceable obligation handles them?
```

The answer should produce a layer-specific architecture, not a slogan.

## Examples

Defense:

```text
Operational command: centralized.
Civilian oversight, audit, procurement transparency, veteran support, and public accountability: hybrid/distributed where security constraints allow.
```

Security:

```text
Coercive police power: centralized or tightly bounded under law.
Prevention, victim support, non-coercive community observation, evidence production, and institutional accountability: distributable under safeguards.
```

Health:

```text
Public guarantee of urgent care, patient rights, triage standards, coverage rules, accreditation, financing rules, and health data interoperability: central or shared legal framework.
Urgent care service provision: distributable among eligible public hospitals, private clinics, university hospitals, nonprofits, cooperatives, concession-like operators, or other accredited providers under the same public rules.
Prevention projects, local clinics, care services, patient evidence, fiscalization, and performance visibility: hybrid/distributed.
```

If a private clinic provides state-financed emergency care, the distributed layer is service provision, not absence of rules. If it fails to meet the accepted service commitments, the system may block or retain payments, record non-compliance, affect role-specific reputation, trigger correction, revocation, replacement, guarantee use, or complaint handling. Separately, the same failure may create legal consequences such as administrative sanctions, civil liability, license effects, fines, judicial action, or regulatory intervention where the applicable legal framework provides them.

Sports and culture:

```text
Project financing, local execution, evidence, and fiscalization: highly distributable.
Safety, child protection, public access rules, and anti-fraud controls: common rules and oversight.
```

Bounded resource allocation:

```text
Citizen allocation of the assignable share: distributed.
Aggregate fiscal envelope, non-assignable common pool, legal custody, and macro fiscal stability: centralized or country-implemented coordination.
```

## Relationship to Core v0

Core v0 applies H001 narrowly. It does not redesign every state function. It builds a project-based distributed governance layer for functions where bounded resource allocation, execution, evidence, fiscalization, accountability, delegation, and reputation can be tested without claiming that all authority or all public budgets should be distributed.

Core v0 also separates platform consequences from legal consequences. The system may record facts, block system payments, affect reputation, activate complaints, require correction, or route issues to competent review. It does not invent legal jurisdiction. Legal sanctions, liability, permits, licenses, court orders, administrative fines, or enforceable service obligations depend on the applicable law, contract, regulator, court, or competent authority.

H001 remains the broader analytical principle used to decide which functions may later be opened, tutored, suspended, kept centralized, or treated as hybrid.

## Status

Core framing hypothesis aligned with the v0 functional matrix, bounded distributed allocation, public-authority/operator boundary, operating modes, and project-based scope freeze.

Needs literature review and a more formal typology of functional layers, risk factors, and distribution conditions before becoming a final theory.
