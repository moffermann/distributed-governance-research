# H002 — Distributed Resource Allocation

## Hypothesis

When the main problem is identifying and supporting valuable social projects, distributed allocation of resources may outperform centralized allocation because it aggregates more local knowledge, preferences, and observed outcomes.

## Value definition

Value is not limited to economic return. Value can include:

- better education;
- better care for the elderly;
- improved health outcomes;
- poverty reduction;
- cultural enrichment;
- community strengthening;
- scientific discovery;
- reduction of suffering;
- increased opportunities.

## Key distinction

A distributed system does not require an algorithm to objectively measure social value. The algorithm can coordinate human valuations. The central question is who expresses value judgments: a small group or millions of people.

## Mechanism

Citizens allocate part of their resources to projects or institutions they consider valuable. Organizations that demonstrate results attract support. Organizations that fail lose support or become visible as needing intervention.

## Status

Promising hypothesis. Requires design of safeguards for neglected causes, information asymmetry, emotional manipulation, and funding volatility.
