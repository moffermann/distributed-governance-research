# H004 — Institutional Monopoly Reduction

## Status

Core framing hypothesis aligned with [[../concepts/public-authority-operator-boundary-v0|Public Authority / State-Owned Operator Boundary v0]] and [[../../docs/43_PUBLIC_INSTITUTION_EXCLUSION_AND_C007_RESOLUTION|C007]].

## Hypothesis

Many failures attributed to public institutions are not caused merely by public ownership, but by monopoly status, weak accountability, opacity, and lack of contestability.

Reducing institutional monopoly should therefore mean reducing opaque, non-accountable, non-contestable control over public functions, not automatically excluding every publicly owned operator from service delivery.

## Rationale

A public institution or monopoly provider that performs poorly often continues receiving budget, authority, or protected revenue. Poor service may persist for years because users, funders, beneficiaries, and affected parties cannot easily redirect support, trigger replacement, or force transparent accountability.

Distributed governance attempts to change this incentive structure:

- projects must declare value floors and antivalue ceilings;
- operators must accept metrics, evidence, fiscalization, and disbursement conditions;
- performance becomes traceable through closure, responsibility, and reputation records;
- poor performance can affect future financing, revocation, replacement, or concession renewal;
- citizens and funders can compare actors by reviewed role performance.

## Important nuance

This hypothesis is not anti-institution, anti-public, or automatically pro-private.

The central distinction is functional:

```text
Public authority:
  exercises legal, regulatory, budgetary, eligibility, admissibility,
  tutored, fiscal, or coercive power.

Operator:
  provides a service, executes a project, runs infrastructure, or delivers
  a public-value commitment under accountability rules.
```

Private companies may provide public services with public funding. Public enterprises may operate like service providers. Civic organizations may execute public-value projects. What matters is whether the actor is subject to contestability, auditability, fiscalization, evidence, guarantees, revocation, and reputation, or whether it is protected by opaque authority.

## Three monopoly patterns

### 1. Competition in the market

Multiple actors can serve users or projects at the same time.

Examples:

- several sports program operators;
- several school-supply providers;
- several elder-care providers;
- several fiscalizers or evidence producers selected through protocolized rules.

This is the easiest case for distributed funding and performance comparison.

### 2. Competition for the market

Only one operator may be practical in a territory or function, but that operator can be selected, audited, penalized, replaced, or renewed through transparent conditions.

Utilities are the key example.

Parallel water, sewer, electricity, or other network infrastructure may be inefficient or impossible. The better model may be concession-like competition for the right to operate, with strict value floors, antivalue ceilings, service-quality obligations, independent evidence, penalties, revocation, and replacement.

### 3. Monopoly of public authority

Some functions require public authority or strong centralized coordination, especially where force, legal coercion, territorial sovereignty, criminal sanctions, or binding adjudication are involved.

These functions may remain centralized or externally authoritative, but their decisions should still become more transparent, auditable, and bounded where possible.

## Utility example

A sanitary company may have a territorial monopoly because duplicating water networks is impractical.

The failure mode is not simply that the operator is public or private. The failure mode is that the operator has:

- guaranteed revenue without performance consequences;
- weak or opaque quality evidence;
- slow or captured oversight;
- no credible replacement path;
- political protection or loss socialization;
- no role-specific reputation pressure.

Distributed governance can partially address this even when a single territorial operator remains:

```text
Service values:
  continuity, water quality, response time, coverage, affordability.

Antivalues:
  outages, contamination, service inequity, environmental harm,
  excessive unresolved complaints.

Evidence:
  sensor records, service logs, user reports, independent audits,
  regulator records, fiscalizer review.

Consequences:
  correction, retained payments, fines where applicable, reputation effects,
  concession review, revocation, or replacement.
```

The key is not multiplying pipes. The key is making monopoly operation accountable, contestable for renewal or replacement, and visible.

## Relation to C007

C007 now distinguishes public authorities from state-owned operators.

In tutored mode, a public authority cannot review admissibility, define scope, or control approval while its own controlled operator competes in the same scope.

In non-tutored or open mode, a state-owned operator may participate if it is treated as an ordinary eligible organization, declares ownership/control relationships, and receives no privileged approval, fiscalization, disbursement, subsidy, immunity, or regulatory advantage over other operators.

## Potential benefits

- better incentives for public, private, civic, and public-enterprise operators;
- faster replacement of poor performers;
- visibility of failing institutions or operators;
- quality competition instead of political access;
- stronger accountability to users, funders, beneficiaries, and affected parties;
- clearer distinction between authority functions and service-delivery functions;
- safer handling of natural monopolies through concession-like accountability.

## Failure modes

- replacing a public monopoly with a private monopoly without improving accountability;
- allowing a public authority to protect its own operator in tutored mode;
- hidden subsidies or treasury backstops that make competition unequal;
- operators gaming metrics while degrading unmeasured service quality;
- fragmentation of functions that require unified command or legal authority;
- revocation without continuity planning for essential services.

## Safeguards

- ownership and control declarations;
- related-party and authority-link review;
- independent fulfillment/control evidence;
- fiscalization separated from operator control;
- value floors and antivalue ceilings;
- financial assurance and retention where applicable;
- role-specific reputation and performance history;
- concession, replacement, or continuity rules for essential services;
- public Governance Resolutions for tutored decisions;
- clear distinction between operating a service and exercising public authority.

## Scope classification

Core v0 framing hypothesis.

The full legal design of concessions, tariff regulation, sector-specific sanctions, public-enterprise governance, and utility replacement belongs to country implementation and later formal modeling.

## Principle

> The target is not public ownership. The target is opaque monopoly power without accountability, contestability, evidence, and consequence.
