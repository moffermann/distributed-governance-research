# H011 — Financial Assurance and Project Revocability

## Hypothesis

Distributed funding systems require financial assurance and revocability mechanisms. Transparency and reputation are not enough to prevent fraud, abandonment, misrepresentation, or harmful execution.

## Rationale

If institutions, companies, foundations, or projects compete for resources, they have incentives to overpromise, hide negative externalities, manipulate results, or misuse funds.

A project may declare that it will do A, receive resources, and then do B. In extreme cases, it may misappropriate funds or abandon execution.

Therefore, trust must be supported by enforceable mechanisms, not only by social approval.

## Core mechanisms

A distributed funding architecture should include some combination of:

- guarantees or performance bonds;
- insurance mechanisms;
- retained funds;
- milestone-based disbursement;
- escrow-like custody;
- public fulfillment/control evidence requirements;
- continuous community supervision;
- blocking or revocation mechanisms;
- financial penalties;
- reputation penalties;
- legal or arbitral remedies.

## Updated project cycle

The project cycle should include assurance before funding and supervision during execution:

```text
proposal
→ declared goals
→ declared negative externalities
→ financial assurance / guarantee
→ financing
→ milestone-based execution
→ continuous supervision
→ evidence
→ evaluation
→ progressive fund release
→ reputation and future funding
```

## Externalities

Projects should declare known negative externalities. The system should not necessarily prohibit projects automatically when externalities exist. Instead, it should make them visible so society can decide whether the expected value justifies the cost.

## Social supervision

Community supervision is a central part of the architecture. Communities often react strongly to projects they consider harmful, even when they are not directly financing them. If citizens are allocating resources directly, their incentive to supervise should increase.

## Principle

> Trust is not sustained by transparency alone. It requires transparency, continuous supervision, and enforceable guarantees.

## Open questions

- Which project categories require performance bonds?
- What guarantee level is proportional to project risk?
- Who can trigger suspension or revocation?
- What evidence threshold is required to block a project?
- How are malicious complaints or sabotage prevented?
- Who arbitrates disputes over revocation?
