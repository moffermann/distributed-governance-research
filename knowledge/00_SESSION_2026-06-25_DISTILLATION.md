# Session Distillation — Initial Distributed Governance Conversation

Date: 2026-06-25

## Current v0 alignment note

This document is a historical distillation. It should now be read through the accepted H001-H003 refinements:

- H001: distribution is assessed by functional layer, not by whole state function, ministry, policy sector, or public/private label. A centralized public guarantee can coexist with distributed service provision.
- H002: distributed allocation means a configured assignable public-purpose share inside planning scopes, eligibility rules, phase gates, evidence, fiscalization, and common-pool protections. It does not make all public budgets citizen-assignable.
- H003: distributed trust means incentive-compatible trust. It is not blockchain by default, crowd faith, or registry integrity alone; it requires role incentives, verifiable claims, contextualized evidence, contradiction paths, responsible review, consequences, and auditability.

## Origin of the conversation

The initial question was whether, considering only technical feasibility and projecting into the future, artificial intelligence and modern tools could reduce the state to a minimal expression or even make it disappear.

The conversation then evolved from a broad question about the state into a more precise theory about distributed governance, distributed trust, and functional institutional architecture.

## Major evolution of the idea

### 1. From “state disappearance” to functional decomposition

The first major move was to stop treating the state as a single object. Instead, the state was decomposed into functions:

- rule creation;
- dispute resolution;
- security;
- defense;
- resource allocation;
- education, health, culture, sports, science;
- registries and records;
- administration;
- coordination.

This led to the insight that different functions may require different degrees of centralization or distribution.

### 2. From technology as automation to technology as coordination

At first, technology appeared as a way to automate bureaucracy. Later, the deeper point emerged: technology allows coordination without requiring the same degree of central authority.

Relevant tools discussed:

- identity digital;
- voting platforms;
- artificial intelligence;
- cryptography;
- blockchain-like auditability;
- distributed ledgers;
- reputation systems;
- arbitration mechanisms;
- platforms similar to social networks.

### 3. From algorithm as decider to algorithm as coordinator

A key distinction emerged in debate with Ricardo:

- A central algorithm that decides for everyone recreates concentration of power.
- A coordination algorithm can simply aggregate and execute millions of individual decisions.

Analogy: a social network does not decide which post a person likes, comments on, or shares. It coordinates and records millions of individual interactions. In a governance platform, posts could be replaced by projects and likes by resource assignments.

### 4. From value as economic return to value as human/social value

The project must define value broadly. Value is not only monetary. A project generates value if it educates better, cares better, saves lives, reduces poverty, strengthens culture, improves a community, or solves a meaningful human problem.

A distributed allocation system does not need a central algorithm to objectively measure value. It allows value judgments to be expressed by many people rather than by a small committee.

### 5. From institutional elimination to institutional monopoly reduction

The theory does not necessarily eliminate institutions. It challenges institutional monopolies.

A school, hospital, foundation, arbitration provider, cultural center, or research organization may still exist. The key difference is that legitimacy and funding would depend more on demonstrated value, transparency, and citizen support than on monopoly status or centralized budget allocation.

### 6. From state versus anarchy to architecture

The conversation repeatedly encountered the word “anarchy.” The refined position is not chaos, absence of rules, or absence of institutions. It is better described as a new architecture of distributed governance.

Rules still exist. Civil order still exists. Institutions still exist where necessary. What changes is how authority, trust, information, and resource allocation are distributed.

### 7. From blockchain as product to distributed trust as principle

Blockchain appeared not as the solution, but as a useful analogy. The deeper principle is distributed trust:

- trust in information should come from verification, traceability, and multiple sources;
- trust in institutions should come from auditability and results;
- trust in governance should come from distributed participation and transparent processes;
- trust in agreements should be reduced to structured incentives, evidence, and arbitration.

Current H003 later refines this: trust is not created by believing many actors. It is created when role-specific incentives, material claims, contextualized evidence, contradiction paths, review responsibility, consequences, and auditability are connected.

## Functional classification discovered

A key architectural principle emerged and was later refined by H001:

> The degree of distribution should depend on the functional layer being analyzed, not only on the broad state function, ministry, or policy sector.

### Defense

Defense likely requires strong centralization because it involves territorial defense, unified command, doctrine, and the risk that fragmentation of force could lead to conflict or civil war.

### Security

Security may allow functional distribution, not necessarily territorial distribution. The core coercive police role may remain centralized or tightly coordinated, but specialized institutions could complement it:

- drone monitoring;
- AI-supported crime analysis;
- cybercrime units;
- forensic digital services;
- missing persons search;
- specialized surveillance and prevention tools.

The risk is conflict over use of force, so roles must be clearly defined.

### Justice

Justice has substantial distribution potential. Private arbitration and mediation already exist. The key problem is legitimacy and jurisdiction: parties must know in advance which tribunal or arbitration institution applies to which kind of conflict, and all must operate under a shared legal framework.

### Legislative function

The legislative function has strong distribution potential because it does not directly require the use of force. Lawmaking is deliberation, drafting, knowledge aggregation, and decision-making. Technology could allow citizens, specialists, or multiple legislative teams to propose, refine, critique, and vote on laws.

### Executive resource allocation

Many ministries in the executive branch exist primarily as centralized resource allocators: education, health, culture, sports, science, development. The theory suggests that this allocation function could be drastically reduced or replaced by distributed allocation mechanisms where citizens direct a configured assignable share toward eligible public-purpose projects or organizations that demonstrate value under planning, evidence, fiscalization, and common-pool constraints.

## Strongest principle so far

> This is not a debate about more state or less state. It is a functional architecture question: where does concentration produce more benefits than costs, and where does distribution produce better results?

## Open research direction

The project should now formalize:

- function categories;
- distribution criteria;
- risks of distribution;
- mechanisms of accountability;
- examples from existing systems;
- boundaries where centralization remains justified.
