# Literature Scout Skill

## Purpose

Use this skill when searching, reading, or organizing academic literature related to the Distributed Governance Research project.

The goal is not to find sources that confirm the theory. The goal is to map what already exists, what contradicts the theory, and what appears genuinely original.

## Core rule

Do not claim originality until related literature has been searched and mapped.

## Search domains

Prioritize:

- academic papers;
- books;
- working papers;
- institutional reports;
- official documentation for real-world systems;
- credible preprints.

Avoid relying on blog posts unless they point to primary sources.

## Relevant fields

Search across:

- polycentric governance;
- network governance;
- digital governance;
- e-government;
- public choice theory;
- institutional economics;
- mechanism design;
- liquid democracy;
- participatory democracy;
- deliberative democracy;
- blockchain governance;
- DAOs;
- arbitration and private dispute resolution;
- trust infrastructure;
- AI-assisted deliberation;
- algorithmic governance;
- public sector accountability.

## Workflow

1. **Define the research question**
   - What part of the theory are we investigating?

2. **Search broadly first**
   - Gather candidate terms, authors, schools, and concepts.

3. **Identify clusters**
   - What literature family does each source belong to?

4. **Summarize each source**
   - Main claim.
   - Relevance.
   - Agreement with our theory.
   - Tension or contradiction.
   - Concepts worth importing.

5. **Map originality carefully**
   - What does the source already cover?
   - What does it not cover?
   - Does our theory combine components in a new way?

6. **Persist notes**
   - Put source notes in `research/`.
   - Update `research/literature-map.md`.

## Literature note template

```md
# [Title]

## Citation

## Main claim

## Relevance to this project

## Agreements

## Tensions / objections

## Concepts to import

## Open questions
```

## Warning

Do not cherry-pick. A paper that weakens the theory is more valuable than a weak source that supports it.
