# D009 - Defense Against A009: Inequality of Participation

## Attack reference

- Attack file: `attacks/A009-inequality-of-participation.md`
- Attack title: `A009 - Inequality of Participation`
- Roadmap source: Phase 3 priority objection, inequality of participation.

## Attack summary

The attack argues that people with more time, organization, digital ability, money, education, or confidence may dominate funding, objections, evidence, complaints, and delegation. In Macul, sports clubs may mobilize quickly while older adults or disabled affected parties never see the project or object before funding closes.

## Objective evaluation

- Classification: founded and socially important.
- Why it is founded: formal openness does not create equal participation capacity.
- Why it is not fatal to the architecture: the model already includes layered citizen simplicity, participation-support projects, delegation, protected identity, assisted deliberation, source-visible summaries, and auditability.
- Difference of judgment: medium. Some remedies depend on broader social policy and country implementation.
- Editorial distortion risk: high if the attack concludes that distributed participation is invalid unless participation is perfectly equal. The correct standard is comparative: does the system improve visibility, contestability, support, and accountability versus the opaque baseline?

## Response

The defense is that participation capacity must be treated as a system function, not assumed from formal openness.

The project does not rely only on "anyone can participate." It allows participation-support projects, simple citizen-facing layers, delegation, automatic allocation profiles, protected identity for sensitive actions, assisted deliberation, and deeper audit layers for those who want to inspect more.

For Macul, a support project could help older residents read project sheets, upload observations, understand delegation, file complaints, or review accessibility commitments. That support project must not become hidden delegation purchase or political capture.

## Bibliographic basis

- Sherry Arnstein, "A Ladder of Citizen Participation" (1969): participation can be symbolic unless citizens have real influence.
- Archon Fung, "Varieties of Participation in Complex Governance" (2006): who participates and how participation connects to authority matters.
- James Fishkin, `When the People Speak` (2009): deliberative participation requires designed inclusion.
- Mancur Olson, `The Logic of Collective Action` (1965): organized groups can outperform diffuse publics.
- Robert Dahl, `Polyarchy` (1971): broad participation and contestation are both democratic requirements.

## Proposed improvements

Add participation-equity indicators:

- affected-party gap signal for high-impact projects;
- participation concentration by territory, beneficiary group, or delegate cluster;
- participation-support funding visibility;
- warnings when support providers also receive delegation or recommend projects;
- protected-identity usage and abuse monitoring;
- citizen-facing "missing affected voices" prompt where relevant.

## Applies to

- citizen interface layers;
- funding, support, objection, complaint, and evidence flows;
- participation-support projects;
- protected identity;
- delegation and automatic allocation;
- affected-party channels.

## Defense strength and residual risk

Defense strength: moderate to strong. The architecture recognizes participation inequality, but full inclusion cannot be solved by platform design alone.

Residual risk: digitally skilled, organized, or well-funded actors may still dominate. The system should measure and expose that dominance rather than pretend open access is enough.

## Decision

The attack is founded. It does not defeat the project, but it requires participation-equity indicators and anti-capture rules for participation-support work.
