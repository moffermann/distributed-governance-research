- Source: second-round attack queue, intertemporal myopia and long-term underinvestment.

## Attack summary

The attack argues that monthly allocation cycles reward visible near-term outputs and that renewal windows re-expose long-horizon commitments to each cycle's shifting attention, so hyperbolic discounting causes maintenance, prevention, and intergenerational infrastructure to lose to ribbon-cutting projects. Where A006 classifies and protects the continuity risk of obligations that already exist, A032 targets the formation bias operating before any obligation is created: the systematic tilt of new funding away from long-horizon value, so long-term projects are rarely proposed, rarely funded, and never accumulate into the obligations A006 would later protect. Future citizens, who cannot allocate, delegate, object, or complain, are the beneficiaries with the largest stake and no voice.

In the example, a commune's stormwater and slope-stabilization needs require a fifteen-year investment whose benefit is a landslide that never happens. Month after month citizens fund visible plazas, courts, and lighting; no one proposes the slope-stabilization project because it cannot close a funding window against such competition, and future residents have no vote. A decade later a slope fails, and the value the long-horizon investment would have protected is gone without ever having been a funded obligation to protect.

## Objective evaluation

- Classification: partially founded.
- Why it is founded: for new long-horizon value above the floor, formation bias is real, and future citizens are unrepresented in any funding attempt, which no observability layer fixes.
- Why it is not fatal to the architecture: the goods most vulnerable to myopia are shielded structurally rather than behaviorally, through the non-assignable pool, essential floors, and continuity-risk classification that generates continuity-need Ideas before funded periods expire.
- Difference of judgment: medium. The disagreement is over how much long-horizon value is left to unstructured monthly attention once floors, continuity classification, and planning scopes are accounted for.
- Editorial distortion risk: medium. The attack distorts the project if it treats citizen myopia as strictly worse than the electoral myopia it replaces without evidence, since incumbent politicians also operate on a four-year horizon with less visibility of deferred maintenance.

## Response

The defense is that the goods most vulnerable to myopia are protected by structure rather than trusted to citizen attention, while conceding that new long-horizon value above the floor faces real formation bias and that future citizens have no vote. The public budget boundary and essential floors remove catastrophic long-horizon obligations from monthly popularity, and the continuity project rule requires recurring, continuity-critical, and maintenance-dependent projects to expose the funded service period, renewal trigger, and wind-down rule. The continuity-risk classification exists precisely because attention cannot be trusted with these goods: it requires projects to declare whether they are one-time or maintenance-dependent, opens a continuity renewal window before the funded period ends, and surfaces the continuity need as an Idea before interruption rather than after failure. The monthly cycle itself carries the continuity label and can route eligible allocations toward follow-on, maintenance, and mitigation work instead of only toward new outputs.

Under P001, the comparison baseline is not a patient planner. Electoral cycles give incumbent politicians a four-year horizon, and in current systems continuity funding is routinely reduced, delayed, or cancelled even under the same government when priorities change, creating discretion without visibility, traceability, or beneficiary-centered planning. The current baseline is also myopic, with less visibility of deferred maintenance than the architecture provides; the honest claim is not that citizen attention is patient but that it is at least made legible.

For the commune, the defense concedes the sharp edge: a fifteen-year slope-stabilization project whose benefit is a non-event can lose the monthly contest to visible works, and no observability represents the future residents who would benefit. Where the need is a maintenance or continuity obligation, continuity classification can pull it into a protected renewal path; where it is genuinely new long-horizon value above the floor, the architecture can define long-horizon lanes in Planning Scopes and expose the share of funding by time-to-benefit horizon, so the under-funding of durable value is measurable and arguable before the slope fails rather than diagnosed afterward. Funding windows and phases can represent multi-year commitments, though the defense concedes that renewal re-exposure is real and that insulation from per-cycle attention is an improvement still to be built.

## Bibliographic basis

- Alan Jacobs, `Governing for the Long Term` (2011): democracies underinvest where present costs buy future benefits, which is why the architecture shields long-horizon goods structurally rather than trusting present attention.
- David Laibson, "Golden Eggs and Hyperbolic Discounting" (1997): decision-makers over-weight immediate payoffs, so multi-cycle or endowment lanes insulated from per-cycle attention are the proposed commitment device.
- Ted O'Donoghue and Matthew Rabin, "Doing It Now or Later" (1999): present-biased agents procrastinate on deferred-benefit actions such as maintenance, which continuity classification pulls forward through renewal windows.
- Jonathan Boston, `Governing for the Future` (2016): anticipatory governance needs deliberate commitment devices, which long-horizon Planning Scope lanes and horizon-weighted defaults are meant to provide.
- Mancur Olson, `The Logic of Collective Action` (1965): diffuse future interests are under-represented against concentrated present ones, so future-interest observability at least represents them as information in each relevant funding attempt.

## Proposed improvements

Add long-horizon protection and intertemporal observability:

- a time-horizon classification for projects and Planning Scopes by benefit horizon and payoff type, making long-horizon under-funding measurable;
- long-horizon lanes and multi-cycle or endowment funding not fully re-exposed to allocation attention at every renewal;
- a transparent, versioned default weighting toward prevention and maintenance categories presented as a public protocol choice;
- future-interest observability exposing expected long-run value and deferred-harm-averted estimates in relevant funding attempts.

## Applies to

- Citizen funding and automatic allocation profiles;
- monthly allocation cycle;
- continuity risk classification;
- Planning Scope;
- administrative observability.

## Defense strength and residual risk

Defense strength: strong for maintenance and continuity obligations, which continuity classification and floors pull into protected renewal paths; moderate for genuinely new long-horizon value above the floor, where formation bias operates before any obligation forms.

Residual risk: present-biased citizens may still decline to propose or fund fifteen-year prevention, and future beneficiaries remain structurally voiceless in every funding attempt. Observability and horizon-weighted defaults surface and partly counter the bias, but they do not give the future a vote, and insulation of long-horizon lanes from per-cycle attention is not yet built.

## Decision

The attack is partially founded. Phase 3 should add long-horizon lanes in Planning Scopes and intertemporal allocation observability by time-to-benefit horizon, while noting that continuity-critical goods are already shielded and that the electoral baseline the model replaces is itself myopic with less visibility.
