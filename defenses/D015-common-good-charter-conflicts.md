## Attack reference

- Attack title: `A015 - Common-Good Charter Conflicts`
- Roadmap source: Phase 3 priority objection, common-good charter conflicts.

## Attack summary

The attack argues that Core v0 may underprotect common goods because full common-good charter governance is Extension v1+. In Macul, courts may improve sports access but reduce green area, increase noise, displace informal users, or restrict shared public space before a full charter exists.

## Objective evaluation

- Classification: founded but bounded.
- Why it is founded: projects can affect parks, plazas, schools, rivers, ecosystems, heritage, infrastructure, or shared spaces before a full charter system exists.
- Why it is not fatal to the architecture: C022 already separates full common-good governance from Core v0 common-good impact declaration. Core v0 requires affected assets, affected parties, risks, antivalues, evidence, fiscalization, and charter relationship disclosure where relevant.
- Difference of judgment: medium. Some common-good conflicts may require legal, environmental, or cultural authority outside Core v0.
- Editorial distortion risk: high in both directions: importing full common-good charter governance into v0 can overcomplicate the core, while ignoring common-good effects would hollow out accountability.

## Response

The defense is that Core v0 does not need the full common-good charter system to require common-good impact disclosure.

A project can proceed without an active charter, but it cannot hide plausible common-good effects. If a project affects a park, plaza, school, river, cultural asset, shared facility, or ecosystem, it should declare affected assets, affected parties, risks, antivalues, fulfillment/control evidence needs, fiscalization path, and uncertainties.

For Macul, the project should disclose whether it reduces green area, changes public access, creates noise, affects school use, or displaces existing informal users. If uncertain, "uncertain" must have an explanation and follow-up review trigger.

## Proposed improvements

Add a minimum common-good impact sufficiency test:

- identify affected assets and affected parties;
- declare no impact, uncertain impact, or impact with explanation;
- require evidence and fiscalization where impact is plausible;
- require active-charter relationship if a charter exists;
- prohibit unexplained overuse of "uncertain";
- connect material omissions to fiscalization, complaint, mitigation, responsibility, and reputation.

## Applies to

- project creation;
- value-antivalue profile;
- risk and antivalue declaration;
- common-good impact declaration;
- affected-party comments and complaints;
- fiscalization and evidence needs;
- Planning Scope review.

## Defense strength and residual risk

Defense strength: moderate to strong. C022 already answers the main boundary, but sufficiency criteria remain useful.

Residual risk: some common-good harms require legal or technical expertise outside Core v0. The system should surface and route the issue rather than pretend to decide all common-good compatibility.

## Decision

The attack is founded but bounded. The integrated answer is the minimum sufficiency test over existing declaration, evidence, and fiscalization surfaces. The declared limitation is that without a charter system the test relies on declaration honesty plus affected-party and fiscalizer contestation, so underdeclared impacts in communities with weak contestation capacity remain an open exposure.
