# A Literature-Grounded Calibration of the Share of Public Projects with Positive Net Social Value

## Purpose and scope

This note provides a transparent formal basis for calibrating the proportion of public projects that produce positive net social value. It is written to support the use of a central value of 0.65 in the Core v0 analytical framework.

The central claim is deliberately limited:

> A value of 0.65 for the probability that an implemented public project produces positive net social value is a defensible conservative calibration, grounded in large-sample project-evaluation evidence, but it is not an empirically established universal constant.

No available dataset measures the lifetime net social value of every public project across countries, sectors, and institutional environments. The parameter must therefore be understood as a model input derived from the closest available empirical evidence, adjusted downward to reflect external-validity and measurement limitations.

---

## 1. Definition of net social value

Consider a public project \(i\) with an implementation date \(t=0\), a useful life of \(T_i\) periods, and a social discount rate \(r_s>0\).

Let:

- \(B_{it}\) denote the total social benefits generated by project \(i\) in period \(t\);
- \(C_{it}\) denote the total social costs generated by project \(i\) in period \(t\), including investment, operation, maintenance, externalities, and opportunity costs;
- all benefits and costs be valued at social or shadow prices rather than merely at observed financial prices.

The project's net social value is its social net present value:

\[
U_i(r_s)
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_i}\frac{B_{it}-C_{it}}{(1+r_s)^t}.
\]

The project creates net social value if and only if

\[
U_i(r_s)>0.
\]

For a population of implemented public projects, define

\[
p_{U+}
=
\Pr\!\left(U_i(r_s)>0\right).
\]

For a finite sample of \(N\) projects, its empirical counterpart is

\[
\widehat p_{U+}
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\mathbf{1}\!\left\{U_i(r_s)>0\right\},
\]

where \(\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}\) is an indicator equal to one when the condition is true and zero otherwise.

The quantity of interest is therefore not the average return across projects. It is the proportion of individual projects whose discounted social benefits exceed their discounted social costs.

---

## 2. Connection between net social value and the economic internal rate of return

Project-evaluation studies often report an economic internal rate of return, or EIRR, rather than a social net present value.

Define the net social flow

\[
X_{it}=B_{it}-C_{it}.
\]

The EIRR, denoted \(\rho_i\), is a discount rate satisfying

\[
0
=
\sum_{t=0}^{T_i}\frac{X_{it}}{(1+\rho_i)^t}.
\]

Under the conventional investment cash-flow pattern—initial net costs followed by net benefits—and assuming a unique economically meaningful internal rate of return, the social net present value is decreasing in the discount rate. It follows that

\[
U_i(r_s)>0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\rho_i>r_s.
\]

Thus, for projects for which the EIRR is well defined,

\[
p_{U+}
=
\Pr(\rho_i>r_s).
\]

This equivalence is important. A positive EIRR is not, by itself, sufficient to establish positive net social value when society's opportunity cost of capital is positive. The correct threshold is the social discount rate or another defensible social hurdle rate.

The internal-rate-of-return criterion also has known limitations. Non-conventional cash-flow patterns may generate multiple or nonexistent internal rates of return, and mutually exclusive projects should normally be ranked by net present value rather than by EIRR. In the World Bank evidence used below, however, EIRR was the standard project-level economic performance measure, calculated using shadow prices and conventional long-lived investment profiles.

---

## 3. The principal empirical anchor

The closest large-sample evidence located for the present purpose is Pohl and Mihaljek's study of 1,015 World Bank projects.

### 3.1 Dataset and evaluation stage

Pohl and Mihaljek (1992) compared:

1. the economic rate of return estimated when each project was appraised; and
2. the economic rate of return re-estimated when construction had been completed and normal operations began, usually five to ten years after appraisal.

The final sample contained 1,015 projects for which sufficiently complete data were available. Approximately 40% were agricultural projects, 30% transport, 20% energy, and the remainder industry and urban development.

The mean economic rate of return fell from 22% at appraisal to 16% at project completion. This decline is itself material: appraisal estimates were optimistic predictors of completion-stage performance.

### 3.2 Distribution of completion-stage returns

The authors report the following proportions for re-estimated economic rates of return at project completion:

- 25% of projects had a return below 10%;
- 14% had a return below 5%;
- 8% had a zero or negative return.

The complementary proportions are therefore:

\[
\Pr(\rho_i\ge 10\%)\approx 1-0.25=0.75,
\]

\[
\Pr(\rho_i\ge 5\%)\approx 1-0.14=0.86,
\]

and

\[
\Pr(\rho_i>0)\approx 1-0.08=0.92.
\]

The first number is the most useful anchor. Pohl and Mihaljek explain that the World Bank generally financed projects with an appraisal EIRR of at least 10%, and they discuss 10% as a plausible failure threshold in the Little–Mirrlees framework. If \(r_s=10\%\), the conventional EIRR–NPV equivalence gives

\[
\Pr\!\left(U_i(10\%)>0\right)
=
\Pr(\rho_i>10\%)
\approx 0.75,
\]

subject to rounding and the distinction between strict and weak inequalities at the threshold.

This does **not** imply that 92% of projects generated positive net social value at an economically relevant opportunity cost. The 92% figure only establishes that their reported completion-stage EIRR was above zero. With a positive social discount rate, the appropriate empirical anchor is the fraction exceeding that rate. At a 10% hurdle, that fraction is approximately 75%.

---

## 4. Why the empirical anchor cannot be used without adjustment

The 0.75 estimate is informative, but it should not be mechanically treated as the population share for all public projects. Four limitations justify a conservative downward adjustment.

### 4.1 Selection into World Bank financing

The authors explicitly warn that World Bank projects need not be representative of public-sector investment in developing countries. Governments may submit projects with above-average expected returns or below-average risks, and World Bank appraisal and supervision may improve project quality relative to the broader public-project population.

In statistical terms, the observed sample is selected:

\[
\Pr(U_i>0\mid S_i=1)
\neq
\Pr(U_i>0),
\]

where \(S_i=1\) denotes selection into the evaluated World Bank portfolio.

If selection and supervision are positively associated with project quality, then

\[
\Pr(U_i>0)
\le
\Pr(U_i>0\mid S_i=1).
\]

The completion-stage estimate of approximately 0.75 may consequently be an upper benchmark for a wider and more heterogeneous universe of public projects.

### 4.2 Completion estimates are not final lifetime ex post returns

The re-estimated returns were calculated when construction was completed and normal operations began. The authors emphasize that these are not true lifetime ex post rates of return. Some implementation risks were already observed—such as construction cost, delay, and initial operating performance—but future operating costs and benefits still had to be forecast.

Let \(\rho_i^{c}\) denote the completion-stage re-estimate and \(\rho_i^{x}\) the eventual lifetime ex post return. The study observes \(\rho_i^{c}\), whereas the ideal classification would use \(\rho_i^{x}\):

\[
\mathbf{1}\{U_i>0\}
=
\mathbf{1}\{\rho_i^{x}>r_s\}.
\]

Because \(\rho_i^{x}\) remains uncertain at completion, classification error remains possible.

### 4.3 Quantifiability and sector coverage

The analysis includes projects for which economic rates of return could reasonably be quantified. Technical assistance, structural adjustment operations, and other interventions without comparable EIRR estimates were excluded. The estimated proportion therefore applies most directly to capital and infrastructure-like projects with monetizable benefit streams.

It should not be interpreted as direct evidence for every government activity, regulation, transfer program, defense activity, cultural program, or institutional reform.

### 4.4 Measurement and model risk

Economic cost–benefit analysis depends on forecasts, shadow prices, counterfactual assumptions, project boundaries, useful lives, externality valuation, and discounting. A reported EIRR is an estimate rather than a directly observed physical quantity.

Pohl and Mihaljek show that appraisal EIRRs were poor predictors of completion EIRRs and that most projects moved below the 45-degree line relating appraisal and completion returns. This uncertainty supports using a parameter below the raw completion-stage success fraction when calibrating a general model.

## 5. Derivation of the 0.65 baseline calibration

Let

\[
p_W
=
\Pr(U_i>0\mid S_i=1,\,M_i=1),
\]

where:

- \(S_i=1\) denotes selection into the World Bank portfolio;
- \(M_i=1\) denotes that the project's social return is sufficiently quantifiable to calculate an EIRR.

Using a 10% social hurdle rate, the Pohl–Mihaljek evidence gives the empirical anchor

\[
\widehat p_W\approx 0.75.
\]

Let \(\delta\ge0\) denote a conservative external-validity and measurement adjustment accounting jointly for:

1. favorable project selection;
2. potentially stronger appraisal and supervision;
3. the use of completion-stage rather than lifetime ex post returns;
4. exclusion of projects without quantifiable EIRRs; and
5. residual model and valuation error.

Define the general calibration as

\[
p_{U+}^{\mathrm{base}}
=
\widehat p_W-\delta.
\]

Choosing

\[
\delta=0.10
\]

gives

\[
\boxed{
 p_{U+}^{\mathrm{base}}=0.75-0.10=0.65
}.
\]

The ten-percentage-point adjustment is not estimated by Pohl and Mihaljek and must not be represented as though it were. It is a transparent conservative modelling judgment applied to a published empirical benchmark. Its purpose is to avoid importing the strongest observed multilateral-project result unchanged into a broader public-project population.

Equivalently, the adjustment reduces the observed benchmark by approximately 13.3%:

\[
\frac{0.75-0.65}{0.75}
\approx 0.133.
\]

This is modest enough to preserve the central implication of the empirical evidence—that a majority of evaluated projects generated returns above a meaningful hurdle—but large enough to acknowledge that the sample is comparatively selected and incompletely ex post.

---

## 6. Recommended interpretation in Core v0

The parameter should be stated as follows:

> Core v0 adopts \(p_{U+}=0.65\) as a conservative baseline probability that an implemented public project produces positive net social value. The calibration is anchored to Pohl and Mihaljek's sample of 1,015 World Bank projects, in which approximately 75% retained a completion-stage economic rate of return of at least 10%. The baseline is reduced by ten percentage points to account for favorable project selection, stronger multilateral appraisal and supervision, incomplete lifetime observation, limited sectoral coverage, and measurement uncertainty. The resulting 0.65 is a modelling calibration, not a universal empirical constant.

The following sensitivity range is recommended:

\[
p_{U+}
\in
[0.50,0.75],
\]

with:

- \(0.50\) as a pessimistic scenario for weak institutional environments or broader project universes;
- \(0.65\) as the conservative central calibration;
- \(0.75\) as the unadjusted completion-stage multilateral-project benchmark at a 10% hurdle rate.

A model result that is intended to be robust should be reported across this interval rather than only at the baseline value.

---

## 7. What this calibration establishes—and what it does not

### It establishes

1. A formal mapping from positive net social value to the EIRR threshold under conventional project cash flows.
2. A large-sample empirical benchmark of approximately 0.75 for projects exceeding a 10% completion-stage economic return.
3. A transparent derivation of a more conservative 0.65 model input.
4. A defensible sensitivity interval that exposes rather than hides parameter uncertainty.

### It does not establish

1. That exactly 65% of all public projects in every country and sector create net social value.
2. That World Bank projects are representative of all public investment.
3. That completion-stage re-estimated returns equal final lifetime ex post returns.
4. That an EIRR above zero is sufficient when the social opportunity cost of capital is positive.
5. That projects with \(U>0\) are necessarily optimal uses of scarce public funds.

The final distinction is essential. A project can have positive net social value and still be inferior to an available alternative. If projects \(i\) and \(j\) are mutually exclusive, both may satisfy

\[
U_i>0,
\qquad
U_j>0,
\]

while

\[
U_j>U_i.
\]

Selecting project \(i\) would then sacrifice \(U_j-U_i\) units of attainable social value. Core v0 should therefore distinguish between the probability that a project is value-creating and the efficiency of project selection relative to the feasible opportunity set.

---

## 8. Reproducible arithmetic

The numerical calibration requires only the following steps:

\[
\begin{aligned}
N &= 1{,}015,\\
\text{share below 10%} &\approx 0.25,\\
\text{share at or above 10%} &\approx 1-0.25=0.75,\\
\text{conservative adjustment} &=0.10,\\
p_{U+}^{\mathrm{base}}&=0.75-0.10=0.65.
\end{aligned}
\]

Because the published percentages are rounded, 0.75 should be treated as an approximate benchmark rather than an exact sample fraction reconstructed from project-level observations.

## 10. Citation-ready paragraph

The following paragraph may be inserted into a paper or methodological appendix:

> We calibrate the baseline probability that an implemented public project produces positive net social value at \(p_{U+}=0.65\). Under conventional investment cash flows, a project's social net present value is positive if its economic internal rate of return exceeds the social discount rate. Pohl and Mihaljek (1992), using 1,015 World Bank projects, report that approximately 25% had a re-estimated economic rate of return below 10% at the completion of construction; hence approximately 75% met or exceeded a 10% economic hurdle. Because World Bank-financed projects may be positively selected and more intensively appraised and supervised than the broader universe of public projects, and because completion-stage returns are not final lifetime ex post returns, we apply a transparent ten-percentage-point conservative adjustment. The resulting 0.65 is used as a modelling calibration rather than asserted as a universal empirical frequency, with sensitivity analysis over the interval \([0.50,0.75]\).
