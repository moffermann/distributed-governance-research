# Candidate Paper Outline

## Working title

Functional Architecture for Distributed Governance: A Framework for Selective Institutional Distribution

## Abstract placeholder

This paper proposes a functional framework for analyzing which state functions may benefit from distributed governance architectures enabled by digital technology, and which functions continue to require central coordination.

## 1. Introduction

- The state is often debated as a single object.
- This produces the false dichotomy of more state versus less state.
- Digital technology changes coordination costs.
- A functional architecture is needed.

## 2. Problem Statement

- Concentration of power.
- Concentration of information.
- Weak accountability of institutional monopolies.
- Centralized resource allocation.

## 3. Related Literature

- Polycentric governance.
- Network governance.
- Digital governance.
- Blockchain as governance infrastructure.
- Participatory and liquid democracy.

## 4. Functional Typology

- Force-oriented functions.
- Conflict-resolution functions.
- Deliberative/rule-making functions.
- Information-processing functions.
- Resource-allocation functions.

## 5. Distribution Principle

The degree of distribution should depend on the nature of the function.

## 6. Application to State Functions

- Defense.
- Security.
- Justice.
- Legislative process.
- Executive resource allocation.

## 7. Distributed Trust and Information Integrity

- Multiple sources.
- Auditability.
- Verification.
- Reputation.
- No single information monopoly.

## 8. Objections and Risks

- Legitimacy.
- Conflict between institutions.
- Neglected services.
- Security.
- Capture.
- Transition.

## 9. Conclusion

The question is not more state or less state, but where centralization remains functionally justified and where distribution can improve accountability, knowledge use, and value creation.
