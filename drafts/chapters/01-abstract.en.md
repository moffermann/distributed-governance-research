## Abstract

Public spending routinely fuses project choice, execution, and self-certification within one hierarchy, concentrating waste, capture, and unaccountable failure. We ask whether a bounded, legally authorized share of that machinery can separate choosing, spending, certifying, and the information driving them while preserving legal authorization and public auditability.

We present Core v0, a fully specified, object-level architecture. Within legally authorized planning scopes, citizens direct a non-withdrawable share of an existing public budget to projects that predeclare value claims, affected parties, milestones, and evidence contracts. Proposal, execution, evidence production, fiscalization, and custody are separated; funds move in tranches against reviewed milestone evidence with retention and guarantees; executors neither choose nor pay inspectors; and every consequential state transition is public.

Its mechanism is credit versus coverage: central ranking rewarded by claimable political credit can underweight diffuse, low-visibility benefits that coverage-routed distributed selection still surfaces, albeit with voice bias. We subjected the mechanism and architecture to adversarial review of 43 attacks over five rounds, integrating each attack or recording it as a bounded limitation. The primary finding is a fair, literature-anchored asymmetric contrast. In the PROBABLE, source-motivated declared reference scenario—not an empirical calibration—coverage-routed distributed selection recovers about 98% of the full-information greedy reference versus the central’s ~44% (≈2.2×; a ~54-point conditional contrast). This direction remains robust across the declared space and realistic degradation of Core v0’s universal budget routing: routing is architectural, whereas effective independent information is not. These are directional, conditional results from a stylized comparative-institutions model, not calibrated field effects. A matched four-cell selection-by-delivery extension places the full architecture about 58.6 points above the status quo on the same reference ([95% conditional Monte Carlo interval +58.0, +59.2]). A three-layer factorial keeps the full Core v0 diagonal positive in every named world, although individual layer attributions reverse in extreme worlds; planning’s direction is anchored while its standalone magnitude is deferred. The central narrowly overtakes coverage only on a near-harmless world after being granted the literature-denied harm-sight; idealizing the distributed arm the same way makes it win by a landslide. The only sensitivity that materially shrinks the gap—never its sign in the declared range—is correlated/common-mode error in the coverage arm.

The literature-anchored extension grounds the central selector’s direction on every axis in evidence, not its magnitude fitted: near-blindness to diffuse harm in the low-visibility long tail (Hayek 1945; Scott 1998; Olson 1965; Bandiera–Prat–Valletti 2009), projection of its own priors and over-rating of visible benefits (Broockman–Skovron 2018; Flyvbjerg et al. 2003), and credit tilt (Mayhew 1974; Arnold 1990). The pre-registered symmetry gate is a narrow, selection-only robustness check at delivery parity: it idealizes the central as an unbiased, competent value-reader with no corruption and better harm detection than the distributed arm, which is handicapped by adverse voice bias. The distributed contrast remains positive but misses the registered rebuild bar under the frozen rule; because this idealization omits delivery and agenda construction, the gate cannot bound Core v0’s architecture-wide value. The contribution is therefore the architecture, the credit-versus-coverage mechanism, and separate conditional findings on selection, verified delivery, and administrative cost—not the large value-per-budget multiplier reported in an earlier version.

Administrative cost does not run against the distributed arm: net-budget accounting is roughly neutral under a conservative low-spread floor and yields a declared, modest advantage when an asymmetric-cost scenario allows the central’s appraisal/prioritization bureaucracy to greatly exceed a near-zero-marginal-fiscalization platform. Elementary propositions give sufficient conditions for incentive-compatible disbursement and collusion-proof fiscalization under independence and corroboration assumptions.

This is an architecture-and-mechanism contribution, not an impact evaluation: no pilot has run; simulations provide conditional model contrasts for selection, delivery, and administrative cost, but their units are uncalibrated and partial-equilibrium and do not identify target-domain treatment effects. Empirical anchors are predominantly infrastructure and procurement—the milestone-verifiable, project-shaped cases where evidence is clearest—while the architecture is general across the broader project-shaped, discretionary domain, not limited to infrastructure; that broader generality is a design claim, not yet empirically validated. The result is a concrete, criticizable, pilotable institutional design and a disciplined account of what its evidence does and does not support.
