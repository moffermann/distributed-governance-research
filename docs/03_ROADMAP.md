# Project Roadmap

## Purpose

This roadmap defines the working path for developing the Distributed Governance Research project from exploratory conversation into a rigorous theory, then into publishable outputs.

The roadmap is intentionally iterative. The project should not rush into a paper before the conceptual framework, hypotheses, objections, and literature map are strong enough.

## Phase 0 — Preserve the Brain

**Status:** in progress

Goal: ensure that important ideas do not remain trapped in chat history.

Deliverables:

- repository initialized;
- research philosophy documented;
- research method documented;
- initial session distillation created;
- first hypotheses created;
- Ricardo review round distilled.

Exit criteria:

- every major idea from the initial conversation has a home in the repository;
- new sessions are regularly distilled into the repository.

## Phase 1 — Stabilize the Core Theory

Goal: define the minimum conceptual framework clearly enough that it can be attacked, refined, and reused.

Deliverables:

- formal definition of distributed governance;
- formal definition of distributed trust;
- formal definition of functional state architecture;
- typology of state functions;
- distribution criteria by function type;
- glossary of key terms.

Key questions:

- What does “distributed” mean in each context?
- What is the difference between coordination and decision-making?
- What is the difference between institutions and institutional monopolies?
- What counts as value beyond economic return?

Exit criteria:

- the theory can be explained in under five minutes without relying on ideological labels;
- the core terms no longer shift meaning across documents.

## Phase 2 — Attack the Theory

Goal: identify where the architecture breaks or becomes dangerous.

Deliverables:

- objection files for each major risk;
- edge-case analysis;
- failure-mode catalog;
- strongest-counterargument document.

Priority objections:

- legitimacy;
- information capture;
- fiscalization failure;
- manipulation of metrics;
- neglected but essential services;
- volatile funding;
- conflict between arbiters;
- use-of-force fragmentation;
- capture by platforms or algorithms;
- inequality of participation.

Exit criteria:

- every major objection has at least one serious response or is explicitly marked as unresolved;
- unresolved objections are not hidden.

## Phase 3 — Literature Review

Goal: locate the project inside existing academic work.

Research areas:

- polycentric governance;
- network governance;
- public choice theory;
- institutional economics;
- mechanism design;
- liquid democracy;
- participatory democracy;
- digital government;
- blockchain governance;
- DAOs;
- arbitration and private justice;
- trust infrastructure;
- AI-assisted deliberation.

Deliverables:

- annotated bibliography;
- literature matrix;
- “what already exists” document;
- “what appears original” document.

Exit criteria:

- the project can state clearly what it borrows, what it extends, and what may be original.

## Phase 4 — Formalize the Framework

Goal: convert the theory into a structured analytical model.

Deliverables:

- functional classification matrix;
- centralization-distribution decision framework;
- institutional taxonomy;
- trust architecture model;
- resource allocation cycle model;
- accountability cycle model.

Candidate core principle:

> The question is not more state or less state. The question is which functions benefit from centralization and which benefit from distribution, based on their functional nature.

Exit criteria:

- a reader can apply the framework to a state function and understand why it should be centralized, distributed, or hybrid.

## Phase 5 — Write the First Working Paper

Goal: produce a serious first academic-style draft.

Candidate title:

**Functional Architecture for Distributed Governance: A Framework for Selective Institutional Distribution**

Expected structure:

1. Introduction
2. Problem statement
3. Literature review
4. Functional typology of state functions
5. Distribution principle
6. Application to defense, security, justice, legislation, and resource allocation
7. Distributed trust and accountability
8. Objections and limitations
9. Conclusion

Exit criteria:

- the paper presents a clear research question;
- it identifies related literature;
- it states a contribution;
- it distinguishes hypothesis from evidence;
- it acknowledges limitations honestly.

## Phase 6 — External Review

Goal: expose the framework to serious critique before publication.

Reviewers may include:

- technologists;
- economists;
- political scientists;
- lawyers;
- public policy experts;
- AI researchers;
- institutional design researchers;
- practitioners in public administration.

Deliverables:

- review feedback log;
- response-to-reviewers document;
- revised paper draft.

Exit criteria:

- the strongest external objections have been incorporated or answered.

## Phase 7 — Publication Strategy

Goal: decide how to publish based on maturity and audience.

Possible paths:

- working paper;
- arXiv or SSRN-style preprint;
- long-form essay;
- academic journal submission;
- conference paper;
- public-facing explainer;
- book-length project later.

Exit criteria:

- target format chosen;
- audience defined;
- manuscript adapted to that audience.

## Phase 8 — Practical Experiments

Goal: identify small systems that test parts of the theory.

Candidate experiments:

- trust platform for agreements;
- distributed resource allocation prototype;
- project accountability dashboard;
- AI-assisted deliberation interface;
- arbitration selection workflow;
- institutional performance registry.

Exit criteria:

- at least one testable prototype or case study is defined.

## Operating Rule

After every significant conversation or review round:

1. distill new concepts;
2. create or update hypotheses;
3. record objections;
4. update open questions;
5. revise the roadmap if needed.
