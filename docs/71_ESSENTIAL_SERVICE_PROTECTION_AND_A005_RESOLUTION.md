## Resolution decision

A005 is founded and does not distort the project when it preserves bounded distributed allocation and compares the model against real institutional allocation, not against an ideal central state. Central political discretion can also interrupt continuity, hide underfunding, and keep low-priority services underfinanced for years when a government changes agenda.

The project rejects two distorted conclusions:

- all public services must be centrally operated;
- all public budgets or essential guarantees should be citizen-assignable.

The accepted resolution is an `Essential Service Protection` test for Planning Scopes, funding eligibility, and planning-continuity targets. The test is a floor-and-lane rule:

```text
protected essential floor
+ distributed service provision lane
+ visible planning continuity target
```

## Rule added to Core v0

Planning Scopes should identify whether a project or scope affects essential guarantees, continuity, emergency capacity, redistribution, vulnerable beneficiaries, universal access, or low-visibility groups.

The test should record:

- the essential guarantee floor, where one exists;
- the planning continuity target, planning period, and visible underfunding indicator where the scope involves continuity;
- what layer is public/legal guarantee and what layer is service provision;
- whether the service provision lane is citizen-assignable, default-assigned, reserve-backed, non-assignable, or excluded;
- whether the function belongs partly or wholly to the non-assignable common pool;
- underfunding indicators for low-visibility eligible needs;
- cream-skimming risks and beneficiary-protection conditions.

A change in government, operating authority, protocol, or tutored administrator may revise a planning target only through a public, versioned, auditable Governance Resolution, Administrative Rule Change, or equivalent rule-change object. The change should expose effects on beneficiaries, continuity obligations, existing projects, protected floors, and distributed funding lanes.

## Macul example

Sports infrastructure may be eligible for a sports pilot, but elder-care continuity, disability services, emergency reserves, or rights guarantees should not silently depend on sports-project popularity.

At the same time, elder-care projects should not be hidden merely because the current government has other political priorities. A Planning Scope such as `Older-adult care continuity 2026-2030` may define:

- protected floor: minimum continuity for eligible beneficiaries;
- planning target: no service interruption longer than the configured tolerance and an annual coverage target;
- distributed lane: eligible providers may propose home-care, transport, food support, caregiver support, residence fiscalization, or complementary service projects;
- funding treatment: baseline reserve or common-pool protection plus citizen-assignable or default-assigned project lanes where allowed;
- observability: `underfunded protected scope` if the continuity target is not being met.

For Macul, the sports scope may still finance multi-courts, but the system should also show that older-adult care continuity, disability service access, or emergency capacity are protected planning targets rather than invisible losers of the sports funding cycle.

## Citizen simplicity

Citizens should see simple scope labels such as `eligible for civic allocation`, `protected common-pool function`, `distributed service lane`, `reserve-backed continuity need`, `planning continuity target`, `underfunded protected scope`, or `not civic-assignable`.

## Transparency and accountability effect

This prevents two opposite failures:

- authorities or project promoters moving hard obligations into citizen popularity dynamics without visibility;
- authorities hiding or starving continuity needs because the current political agenda deprioritizes them.

The system should make both central political neglect and popularity-driven neglect visible.

## Residual risks

- The exact common-pool boundary is country implementation.
- Authorities may define protected scopes too narrowly or too broadly.
- Low-visibility needs may still require outreach and participation-capacity support.
- Planning targets can become too rigid if revision rules do not allow justified public change.
- Default-assigned or reserve-backed lanes can become hidden allocation if their rules are not public and auditable.

## Integration target

This resolution should inform PlanningScope, ThresholdPolicy, civic wallet scope, common-pool design, default allocation profiles, essential-service pilots, and administrative observability.
