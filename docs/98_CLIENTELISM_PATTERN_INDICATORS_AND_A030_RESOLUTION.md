## Resolution decision

A030 is founded as an adaptation risk. Clientelist machines adapt to new institutions rather than dying with the old ones: brokers migrate in as delegates, participation-support providers, community organizations, or intermediaries, exchanging allocation behavior for private favors arranged entirely off the platform. The quid pro quo is invisible by construction, so relationship graphs and related-party declarations never capture it. This resolution adds pattern-level clientelism indicators while keeping sanction in external law.

Under `knowledge/principles/P007-integrate-or-bound-rule.md`, this resolution integrates observability through the A014 related-party relationship graph and the delegation records rather than adding a new entity or a hard anti-broker ban. Off-platform clientelism attacks the anti-capture claim, so it cannot be ignored, but the on-platform half of the exchange — which today is equally invisible — is already traceable, and the fix is to surface repeated patterns as leads. The off-platform act routes to external law (test 4).

## Rule added to Core v0

The platform makes the on-platform half of a clientelist exchange traceable and surfaces pattern-level indicators over time; it does not adjudicate the off-platform favor, which is named as a distinct risk with a documented observability boundary.

Minimum pattern-level clientelism indicators:

- repeat broker-beneficiary alignments across cycles;
- geographic delegation blocs correlated with related-project funding;
- participation-support providers whose assisted citizens' allocations concentrate on related actors;
- indicators feed review, complaint, and verified-discovery paths as leads, never automatic verdicts, and participation-support projects carry anti-conditioning declarations and independent beneficiary confirmation.

## Macul example

In a peri-urban commune, a funded participation-support operator offers off-platform paperwork help, food parcels, and municipal-job referrals to residents who delegate their sports-scope allocation to it. The platform never sees the food parcel — but the operator's represented weight across four neighborhoods, its delegated-action pattern, and its funding of allied projects are recorded and comparable over time, so a fiscalizer or electoral-integrity authority has a traceable pattern to act on rather than nothing at all.

## Citizen simplicity

Citizens should be able to revoke a delegation privately and immediately for future actions, so they can exit a coercive relationship without the broker observing the exit at action time, and should see that participation help is a separate transparent project, not a hidden delegation fee.

## Transparency and accountability effect

The single street-level exchange stays invisible, but the machine that repeats it thousands of times leaves a statistical signature the platform records — a structural improvement over street-level clientelism, whose allocation side was previously as invisible as its favors.

## Scope boundary and limitation

Off-platform quid pro quo remains invisible by construction; sanction of vote-buying-style exchange is external law, routed to complaint, competent-authority, or electoral-integrity channels. Core v0 imposes no hard anti-broker ban, and community organization and brokerage are not treated as clientelism per se.

Limitation statement: the platform makes machine-style allocation patterns statistically visible over time — a real improvement over street-level clientelism's full invisibility — but a careful machine trading entirely off-platform favors, and splitting influence across multiple entities, remains undetectable from within.

## Residual risks

- The off-platform favor stays unobservable, so pattern indicators produce leads, not proof.
- Brokers can split influence across multiple entities to dampen the visible pattern.
- A genuine participation helper is hard to distinguish from a machine on on-platform data alone, so the platform must not presume the former is the latter.

## Integration target

This resolution should inform delegation and the Delegate role (C019), participation-support projects, the related-party relationship graph (A014), delegated-action reports and administrative observability, and the complaint and competent-authority referral paths.
