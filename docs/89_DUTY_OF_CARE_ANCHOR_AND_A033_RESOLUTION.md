## Resolution decision

A033 is founded. When harm results from the interaction of adequately-performed roles, the responsibility matrix can truthfully find that no role breached its internal obligation, C012 forbids blame by association, and P003 assigns responsibility to "the project," which is not a legal person a victim can sue. The accepted resolution is a `Duty-of-Care Anchor` distinct from the value-focused Primary Responsibility Anchor (H010), plus third-party-harm coverage in the assurance profiles.

Under [[P007-integrate-or-bound-rule|knowledge/principles/P007-integrate-or-bound-rule.md]], this resolution integrates through existing objects — the Financial Assurance Profile, the Post-Closure Coverage Profile, and the responsibility matrix. It names the liable party and provisions coverage; it does not adjudicate liability.

## Rule added to Core v0

Every execution-financeable project names, before disbursement, the actor or actors bearing legal responsibility to third parties for physical and safety harm. The responsibility matrix distinguishes internal role performance from liability to a victim.

Minimum elements:

- a Duty-of-Care Anchor mapping to a solvent, reachable legal person (executor, guarantor, insurer, or designated responsible operator), exposed on the citizen-facing sheet;
- Financial Assurance and Post-Closure Coverage profiles extended to third-party liability coverage proportionate to physical risk where Threshold Policy requires it;
- versioned documentation stating that distributed micro-allocation does not dilute the state's underlying duty of care, and recording where residual public responsibility remains;
- a responsibility-matrix distinction between "no role breached its internal obligation" and "no one is liable to the victim";
- a preserved harm-attribution evidence package — roles, versions, gates, evidence — usable by a court.

## Macul example

In the collapsed-roof scenario, the family's lawyer finds a named defendant with materialized coverage and a documented chain of who did what, instead of "everyone a little, no one enough." The anchor names who owed the child a duty of care; the coverage is sized against third-party physical harm rather than only delivery failure.

## Citizen simplicity

Citizens should see, on each project sheet, a plain statement of who answers for physical harm and that third-party coverage is in force, such as `responsible party: [name] — liability coverage active`.

## Transparency and accountability effect

A victim starts from a named, solvent defendant and a complete attribution record rather than a doctrinal vacuum, and internal role adequacy can no longer be read as no-one-is-liable.

## Scope boundary and limitation

Liability apportionment remains external law. The platform records the duty-bearer and provisions coverage; it does not adjudicate who is at fault or in what proportion.

Limitation statement: a named anchor and coverage guarantee a reachable defendant, not a just outcome, and the doctrinal construction of liability in distributed public funding remains untested in any jurisdiction.

## Residual risks

- Coverage sized to physical risk may still be inadequate to a severe harm.
- A named anchor guarantees reachability, not a correct apportionment of fault.
- The state's residual duty of care in distributed funding is doctrinally unsettled.

## Integration target

This resolution should inform the Primary Responsibility Anchor (H010), the responsibility matrix by role, the Financial Assurance and Post-Closure Coverage profiles, the complaint and audit-trail flows, and the country-implementation legal boundary.
