## Resolution decision

A034 is founded as a characterization gap at the legal interface and does not distort the project when read comparatively: the act it interrogates replaces a state official's discretionary allocation — an act whose formal characterization has never delivered practical, per-decision accountability. The accepted resolution is an allocation-act characterization record carried by the existing Allocation Mandate.

Under `knowledge/principles/P007-integrate-or-bound-rule.md`, this integrates a minimal record through an existing object and bounds the doctrine itself: the platform records the characterization a jurisdiction chooses and discloses it to citizens; constructing that characterization remains external constitutional and administrative law.

## Rule added to Core v0

Every active Planning Scope's Allocation Mandate additionally records how the citizen allocation act is legally characterized in that jurisdiction.

Minimum elements:

- a bounded characterization menu: administrative act, sui generis participation act, or preference input, chosen per country implementation;
- a pilot default: sui generis participation act with vote-like citizen immunity and no individual review right over rule-based reassignments;
- the complaint path preserved, unchanged, for rule violations;
- plain-language disclosure of the characterization on the citizen surface before first allocation;
- a comparator note documenting how the replaced official allocation act is characterized and reviewed in the pilot jurisdiction;
- versioning and audit events on any characterization change, which is a higher-authorization decision like a formula departure.

## Macul example

Before her first allocation, a Macul citizen sees one plain sentence: her act directs public money under the scope's rules, carries no personal liability, and rule-based reassignments (for example on funding-window expiry) are not individually contestable, while rule violations always are. The same screen records which municipal instrument chose that regime.

## Citizen simplicity

One disclosure sentence at first use; no doctrine on the citizen surface. The full characterization, menu, and comparator note live in the scope's public record for lawyers and auditors.

## Transparency and accountability effect

The system replaces an official act whose accountability is formally rich and practically thin with a citizen act whose legal nature is stated in advance and whose every exercise is traced. Neither act's doctrine is the platform's to decide; only one of the two is disclosed and auditable per decision.

## Scope boundary and limitation

Core v0 does not construct, adjudicate, or harmonize administrative-law doctrine, and it does not grant or deny standing: it records the jurisdiction's choice and makes it visible. Characterization menus and their consequences for review rights are country-implementation decisions.

Limitation statement: the platform records the characterization law supplies; it cannot supply one, and no characterization of a citizen allocation act has been tested before any court in any jurisdiction — the doctrinal construction is untested by definition until a pilot produces a dispute.

## Residual risks

- A jurisdiction may impose administrative-act status with full review rights, importing litigation load the architecture did not design for.
- Divergent characterizations across jurisdictions may fragment comparability of pilots.
- The pilot default may be challenged precisely by the organized funders most able to litigate.

## Integration target

This resolution should inform the Allocation Mandate record, Planning Scope, Funding Commitment and reassignment rules, citizen-facing disclosure surfaces, the complaint path boundary, and country-implementation guidance.
