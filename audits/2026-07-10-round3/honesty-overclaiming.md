# Round-3 audit — Honesty & overclaiming

## Verdict

**No — not publication-ready on this dimension.** The governing quantitative-status paragraph is careful, and the paper now correctly calls the adversarial process structured self-critique rather than validation. However, four load-bearing claims elsewhere still exceed or misstate the controlling claim-and-estimand contract. Most importantly, several high-visibility summaries substitute the post-hoc ratio-of-sums for the pre-registered gate statistic, and two sections claim real-world or total-architecture answers that the paper itself says have not been identified.

## Findings

### 1. Major — Gate summaries use the post-hoc 0.026 estimate as though it were the pre-registered decision statistic, and continue to call 0.05 a materiality threshold

- **Exact locators:** `drafts/paper.md:120-124`, `drafts/paper.md:537-552`, `drafts/paper.md:942-947`, `drafts/paper.md:1277-1282`; Spanish mirrors at `drafts/paper.es.md:130-134`, `drafts/paper.es.md:587-603`, `drafts/paper.es.md:1016-1021`, `drafts/paper.es.md:1353-1358`; public companion at `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:5-12` and `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:783-794`; README at `README.md:31`; governing sources at `audits/2026-07-10/symmetry-gate-preregistration.md:41-42`, `audits/2026-07-10/symmetry-gate-preregistration.md:52-62`, and `audits/2026-07-10/symmetry-gate-diagnostics-notes.md:1-10`.
- **What is wrong:** The paper's governing paragraph gets the distinction right: “the pre-registered decision statistic is the pooled median Δ = 0.025” and “a post-hoc ratio-of-sums estimate is Δ = 0.026” (`drafts/paper.md:542-545`). The contribution summary instead says the pre-registered stress test produced “a pooled ~0.026 ... below its 0.05 materiality gate” (`drafts/paper.md:120-123`); Finding 7 again attributes ~0.026 to “the later pre-registered symmetric test” (`drafts/paper.md:942-946`); and the conclusion repeats ~0.026 as the figure below the gate (`drafts/paper.md:1277-1280`). The public companion makes the same substitution twice (`docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:5-12`, `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:783-794`). The frozen pre-registration defines per-world Δ and gates the **pooled median** at 0.05; the ratio-of-sums and its world-cluster interval were computed later and are explicitly post-hoc. Separately, `README.md:31`, `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:8-9`, `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:789-790`, and the two paper summaries call 0.05 a “materiality gate,” although the settled interpretation is a research-program rebuild/no-rebuild threshold on an uncalibrated scale, not policy materiality. Even the contract's shorthand “materiality bar” at `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:63-68` leaves this distinction less explicit than the governing paper paragraph.
- **Why it matters:** This is precisely the provenance distinction the Path-B decision depends on. Using the more polished post-hoc estimate as the gate result falsely upgrades an exploratory diagnostic to confirmatory status. Calling 0.05 “materiality” without the research-program qualifier invites a policy-effect interpretation of an uncalibrated model. These errors occur in the contributions, conclusion, README, and accessible companion—not merely in a technical appendix.
- **Concrete fix:** Standardize every gate summary to: “pre-registered pooled median Δ = 0.025, below the pre-set 0.05 research-program rebuild threshold; NO-GO.” Where useful, add a separate sentence: “A post-hoc ratio-of-sums estimate was Δ = 0.026 with a world-cluster Monte-Carlo interval [0.023, 0.029].” Never compare 0.026 directly to the gate, and replace bare “materiality gate/bar” with “research-program rebuild threshold (not a calibrated policy-materiality threshold)” in the contract and all public summaries.

### 2. Major — The implementation section claims the paper answers whether the architecture improves outcomes “and by how much,” contradicting its own selection-only limitation

- **Exact locators:** `drafts/paper.md:1154-1159` versus `drafts/paper.md:1184-1189`; Spanish mirror at `drafts/paper.es.md:1258-1263`; controlling contract at `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:21-22`, `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:38-44`, and `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:58-68`.
- **What is wrong:** The limitations section correctly says the paper addresses how a stylized *selection* mechanism behaves under delivery parity, “not whether it delivers more value in the world,” and reserves total delivered value for a separately identified future estimand (`drafts/paper.md:1154-1158`). Thirty lines later, the implementation section says the research question—whether the allocation architecture “can be improved with today's technology, and by how much”—“is answered” (`drafts/paper.md:1184-1188`). It supplies no in-model qualifier and does not narrow “by how much” to the small conditional selection contrast.
- **Why it matters:** This resurrects the exact total-effect inference that Path B retired. A reader is told both that real-world delivered value has not been identified and that the improvement and its magnitude have already been answered. No amount of caveating elsewhere cures a direct claim of answer in a publication-facing implementation pathway.
- **Concrete fix:** Replace the sentence with the actual state of knowledge: the paper specifies a buildable architecture and demonstrates a conditional selection-mechanism direction; whether implementation improves real-world delivered value, and by how much, remains for independent review, empirical calibration, and a bounded pilot. Mirror the correction in Spanish.

### 3. Major — E7 turns a conditional apparatus output into a categorical claim about what real-world auditing can and cannot deliver

- **Exact locators:** `drafts/paper.md:938-957` and `drafts/paper.md:1283-1288`; Spanish mirrors at `drafts/paper.es.md:1012-1029` and `drafts/paper.es.md:1359-1365`; countervailing limitation at `drafts/paper.md:1170-1179`; contract at `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:21-22`, `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:43-44`, and `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:58-68`.
- **What is wrong:** The E7 paragraph first states the correct limit: audit evidence parameterizes the baseline leak but does not calibrate the Core v0 treatment effect (`drafts/paper.md:942-947`). It then declares that audit at empirically documented intensity “deters no diversion at all” and that “what real-world detection buys is a smaller lie ... never more delivery” (`drafts/paper.md:948-956`). Those outcomes follow from E7's stipulated incentive threshold, opportunist-cost distribution, and absence of persistent consequences; published audit parameters do not identify a general causal effect of real-world detection. The conclusion repeats the slogan and says persistent consequences are something “the status quo lacks at any audit intensity” (`drafts/paper.md:1283-1288`), an institutional universal not established by the model or cited audit studies.
- **Why it matters:** This crosses the paper's cleanest epistemic boundary: a baseline parameter informed by empirical literature is not an empirically estimated treatment effect. The prose converts a conditional, model-internal result into a real-world causal claim and then elevates it as the sentence that “survives” retirement of the multiplier.
- **Concrete fix:** Keep the mechanism result only with an adjacent apparatus qualifier: “Under E7's stipulated cost distribution and no-memory baseline, the modeled audit intensity did not deter diversion; it reduced the modeled visibility gap.” Remove “what real-world detection buys,” “never more delivery,” and “at any audit intensity.” Present persistent consequences as a design hypothesis for pilot testing, not as an established absence/effect in all status-quo institutions.

### 4. Major — Universal claims about taxation, redistribution, and “any allocation institution” exceed the paper's bounded, non-distributional outcome measure

- **Exact locators:** `drafts/paper.md:30-37`, `drafts/paper.md:108-124`, and `drafts/paper.md:1262-1266`; Spanish mirrors at `drafts/paper.es.md:32-39`, `drafts/paper.es.md:119-134`, and `drafts/paper.es.md:1338-1342`; correct scope at `drafts/paper.md:15`; controlling contract at `research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:46-56`.
- **What is wrong:** The abstract correctly scopes claims to infrastructure-like public investment (`drafts/paper.md:15`). The body nonetheless says “States do not tax to redistribute,” that taking from the rich does not improve the most vulnerable person, and that delivered value per public money is the goal (`drafts/paper.md:30-37`). The contribution calls this “the criterion public budgets exist to serve” (`drafts/paper.md:114-116`), and the conclusion upgrades it to “the right criterion for any allocation institution” (`drafts/paper.md:1262-1264`). The governing contract says the outcome is an aggregate utilitarian-style sum that “says nothing about redistribution or equity” and is limited to a bounded infrastructure-like slice, not the whole budget or the purpose of the state (`research/claim-and-estimand-contract.md:46-53`).
- **Why it matters:** These are not merely ambitious framing choices. They make unsupported universal claims in the very domains—redistribution, equity, and the purpose of taxation—that the model explicitly cannot evaluate. They also make a bounded architecture paper appear to settle a normative public-finance criterion it does not measure.
- **Concrete fix:** Rewrite the opening and conclusion around the bounded domain: for infrastructure-like public-project allocation, one relevant criterion is delivered, verified value per resource, alongside distributional and rights constraints not represented by the model. Delete the claims about why states tax and what improves the most vulnerable person, or support and sharply qualify them as normative premises rather than findings.

### 5. Minor — The accessible companion overstates the participation-decay result as categorical survivability

- **Exact locators:** `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:773-782` and `docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:928-935`; more precise source account at `drafts/paper.md:638-655`.
- **What is wrong:** The companion says “participation decay is survivable exactly where that prioritization layer is strong” (`docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:773-778`) and answers “Does the model depend on high citizen attention? No” (`docs/101_CORE_V0_MINIMAL_PUBLISHABLE_MODEL.md:934`). The underlying paper supports only a bounded simulation result: active evaluation fell from 10% to 2% over 24 cycles; the anchor's share buffered aggregate selection, but within-cycle quality alignment still eroded under all tested conditions (`drafts/paper.md:638-655`). “Survivable” and a categorical “No” suppress those tested ranges, the dependence on an assumed high-quality anchor, and the residual degradation.
- **Why it matters:** Participation resilience is an adoption-critical claim. The public companion turns a conditional buffer demonstrated in one stylized apparatus into a general property of the architecture, beyond the parameter range and outcome the experiment actually tested.
- **Concrete fix:** Say: “In the tested 24-cycle parameter grid, a strong default anchor buffered the aggregate loss from attention falling from 10% to 2%, although within-cycle alignment eroded in every condition; this is a conditional model result, not evidence of deployment resilience.” Make the FAQ answer conditional on anchor quality and the tested apparatus.

## Confirmed clean points

- The main governing paragraph correctly separates the pre-registered median from the post-hoc ratio-of-sums and labels the latter interval as Monte-Carlo uncertainty (`drafts/paper.md:537-552`; Spanish `drafts/paper.es.md:587-603`).
- The abstract correctly reports 18/18 positive primary cells, pooled median Δ = 0.025, NO-GO's threshold failure, uncalibrated units, partial equilibrium, and no identified delivery effect (`drafts/paper.md:13-15`).
- The paper explicitly calls the five-round adversarial process “structured self-critique rather than validation” and acknowledges that a self-administered adversary cannot substitute for independent review (`drafts/paper.md:1037-1042`).
- The full-information greedy reference is not described as an optimum or upper bound in the current headline/gate passages.
